Read the Tide Before the Ripple: A 2018 Lesson in Market Context

+------------------------------------------------------------+

In July 2018, an anonymous trader who would later become known for composite macro reads published a quiet thread on something that looks, at first glance, almost too simple to teach. He drew a diagram of an 8-state trend cycle. He labeled four nested ocean metaphors. He showed a few forex charts with annotated entries. Most readers scrolled past.

Looking back from 2026, that thread reads like the foundation stone of an entire philosophy. Every composite framework that came later, every 4-tier spike detector, every multi-signal stack we still backtest today, started with this one idea: a market move only means something inside its context. The lower timeframe lies to you unless the higher timeframe agrees.

That is the lesson worth keeping. Let's slow down and walk through it carefully.

The Eight States: A Gradient, Not a Switch

Most retail traders see two market states. The line goes up. The line goes down. You buy one and sell the other.

Kerberos opened his 2018 session by listing 8 states instead. He named them with engineering precision: Up Trend, Up Trend Overbought, Up Trend Overbought Reversal, Up Trend Reversal, then the mirror four states for the downtrend. The point of the granularity was simple. An uptrend that has just begun behaves differently from an uptrend that has stretched too far. A reversal that follows an overbought spike behaves differently from a reversal that follows steady accumulation.

For day-to-day decisions he simplified this further into a 4-state model: Up, Up Reversal, Down, Down Reversal. He explained that he trades the slopes. An up slope is bullish. A down slope is bearish. A trend that has flattened and is rolling over is treated as the opposite direction.

The philosophical move here is worth pausing on. Binary thinking compresses information. A market is rarely 0 or 1. It is somewhere between exhausted and accelerating, between healthy and overextended. A trader who only sees two states keeps reacting to noise inside the larger gradient. A trader who builds in even 4 states already has a vocabulary for hesitation.

This habit shows up everywhere later in his work. The 4-tier VIX Spike Detector our team validated in 2025 is structurally the same idea: not on or off, but a graded scale where each tier has a different action. The Max Optimism Stack uses 4 bricks and counts how many are firing. The granularity is the discipline.

The Ocean: Tide, Large Waves, Small Waves, Ripples

The metaphor he drew next is the part most worth memorizing.

The monthly chart is the tide. The weekly is the large wave. The daily is the small wave. The 4-hour is the ripple. Each is composed of the smaller units below it. Twenty-four 1-hour bars build a single daily bar. Roughly 120 hourly bars build a single weekly bar.

When you put a higher-timeframe indicator on a lower-timeframe chart, the higher signal looks like a flat stairstep. It does not move for 24 bars at a time on an hourly chart if you are watching the daily. That visual flatness is not a defect. It is the data telling you where the inertia lives.

The trader who only watches the ripple sees endless movement. Every 15-minute candle feels meaningful. Every breakout looks tradable. The trader who reads the tide first sees something different. The tide either supports the move or fights it. If the monthly is rolling over while the 4-hour is ripping higher, that rip is borrowed time. The wave is sloshing inside a falling pool.

This is the core philosophical claim. Context cascades down. A signal at the ripple level is meaningless without its place in the small wave. The small wave is meaningless without the large wave. The large wave is meaningless without the tide.

You can already feel why this thinking became the embryo of every composite signal. If a single timeframe is too noisy to trust, then a single indicator probably is too. The fix is the same in both cases: stack the contexts and require agreement.

The Trading Rules He Proposed

To make this concrete he listed entry conditions on a 4-hour forex chart. For a long entry he wanted four things at once. The monthly trend up. The weekly trend up. The daily trend up. The 4-hour indicator crossing above the zero line.

That alone would be a useful filter. He added optional confirmations on top. A Fibonacci retracement holding support. A trendline touch. A major daily or weekly support line. A bullish divergence between price and a momentum indicator.

He walked through annotated examples. Long at point A with a bullish divergence and all higher frames aligned. Stop placed 20 pips below the recent low. Target left open with a trailing stop. The trade ran 100 pips. Point B, same setup, 200 pips. Point C, a deeper Fib pullback in alignment, ran past 400 pips.

The asymmetry he was after is the heart of the method. Risk 20 pips. Gain anywhere from 50 to 600. A win rate of 50 percent on that math compounds beautifully. Even a win rate of 30 percent makes money on a long enough sample.

His sample shorts mirrored the longs. Point M, a bearish divergence with all higher frames down. Point F, a downtrending resistance line in alignment. Point G, a bull flag that broke down against a multi-month bearish backdrop. He drew the same conclusion every time. The flag pattern fooled retail traders who were watching only the ripple. The tide had been falling for weeks. The break was telegraphed by the higher frames.

What Survives Our Testing, and What Doesn't

Here is where I owe you the honest part.

The mechanical version of this lesson is multi-timeframe stack alignment as a filter. We coded that filter and applied it to our screeners in 2026. We tested it on 5 live strategies including pullback longs, breakout longs, and reversal setups. We ran it through expanding-window validation with proper out-of-sample splits.

The result was a clean rejection. Zero out of five screeners showed a meaningful improvement when we required higher-timeframe alignment as a hard filter. The Sharpe delta hovered near zero. The win-rate change was inside the noise band. The trades we removed were not systematically worse than the trades we kept.

There is a reason for this, and it is worth understanding. Our screeners already build trend alignment into their construction. A pullback long signal in our system requires the stock to be in an uptrend by design. A breakout signal requires price to be making new highs by design. When you stack an MTF filter on top of an already trend-filtered signal, the filter becomes redundant. It eliminates almost nothing useful and occasionally clips legitimate winners.

The conclusion is delicate. The philosophy is correct. The mechanical implementation as a bonus filter on top of trend-following screeners is redundant.

So what part of the 2018 thread should you keep?

What Truly Transfers

Three pieces of the lesson survive every test we have thrown at them.

The asymmetric R/R discipline. Risk 1 to make 3 or 5 or 10. This is the math that makes a moderate win rate profitable. It is also the math that makes traders profitable when they are wrong about half the time, which most of us are. The 20-pip stop versus 200-pip target ratio he used in 2018 is the same ratio you can use today on equities by anchoring stops to 1.5x the 20-day ATR and letting winners run to 3x or 5x.

Divergence at extremes. Kerberos called bullish divergence the strongest long signal you can find. We have validated this in a narrower form. Composite Divergence in our backtest produced a -0.38 percentage point SPY 10-day excess return after topping signals fired, with a permutation p-value below 0.0001 on 1259 events. SKEW Tail Risk, which is a kind of cross-asset divergence between fear and price, gave a -3.9 percent SPY 60-day reading with similar significance. RSI Breadth at capitulation extremes returned +13 percent SPY at 60 days. The pattern Kerberos described in 2018 as a divergence between price and a higher-timeframe momentum indicator is the same shape we still find statistically when we measure it properly.

The composite philosophy itself. The 2018 thread is the embryonic version of every composite framework he would publish later. Stack contexts. Require agreement. Treat single signals as noise. This idea, applied at the cross-asset level instead of the multi-timeframe level, produced the Max Optimism Stack we backtested at +260 percent versus SPY +160 percent over 8 years. The mechanism is identical. Multiple independent reads of the same underlying condition, each one weak alone, become strong when they line up.

A Worked Example in Modern Form

Imagine you spot a pullback setup on a single stock at the daily chart level. The stock is down 8 percent from a 52-week high. Volume is dropping. The 14-day RSI is at 38. The mechanical signal says buy.

Before 2018 Kerberos, you might pull the trigger here. Single timeframe. Single indicator. Defined stop. It looks clean.

After absorbing the lesson, you ask three more questions. Is the weekly chart still in an uptrend? Is the broader market regime supportive? Is anything in the cross-asset picture warning of stress?

If the weekly is rolling over, the daily pullback is probably the first leg of a larger correction. You pass.

If the weekly is healthy but the market regime shows the VIX above 25 and breadth narrowing, you size smaller. The tide is choppy.

If the weekly is healthy, the regime is calm, and credit spreads are tight, you take the trade at a normal size with a stop at 1.5x ATR below entry and a target near 3x ATR above. The contexts agree. The ripple is moving with the tide.

This is the workflow the 2018 thread teaches when you read past the literal forex setup. The forex examples are scaffolding. The transferable lesson is the layered reading.

Where the Thread Shows Its Age

I want to be honest about the limits. The thread was written by an anonymous trader presenting hand-picked annotated charts. There is no published track record. Survivorship bias in the examples is real. A 2018-vintage 4-state trend indicator applied to 2024 mega-cap dispersion behaves differently from how it behaved on EURUSD in 2017.

His claim of an 80 percent win rate with R/R above 4:1 should be treated with appropriate skepticism. We have never reproduced anywhere close to those numbers under proper out-of-sample validation. The visual cherry-picking of 3 or 4 winning trades on a single chart is an artifact of how trading content gets shared, not evidence of edge.

What remains valuable is the framework of attention. Read up before you read down. Demand agreement before you commit. Define risk before you define reward.

How It Fits a Modern Workflow

In a screener-driven process, the context layer comes first. You filter the universe by regime conditions before you look at any single name. You require multiple confluent signals from independent sources to escalate conviction. You size positions against volatility-aware stops at 1.5x ATR or wider, so a single wrong call never costs more than a small fraction of capital. The 2018 ocean metaphor still does useful work as a sanity check. Before you act on the ripple, look up at the tide.

Key takeaways
  • Markets are nested. The 4-hour chart only means something inside the daily, the daily inside the weekly, the weekly inside the monthly.
  • A binary view of trend (up or down) hides texture. The 8-state cycle adds vocabulary for overbought, exhausted, and reversing.
  • We tested mechanical MTF stack alignment as a filter on 5 screeners and the result was REJECT. The philosophy survives, the redundant filter on already-trended signals does not.
  • What does transfer: asymmetric R/R discipline (risk 20 to make 200), divergence at extremes, and stacking independent reads before committing.
  • Before you trade the ripple, look up at the tide. The signal at the bottom of the stack means what the top of the stack says it means.
