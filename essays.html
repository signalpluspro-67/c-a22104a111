53 Funds Built to Beat the Market. Almost None Beat Doing Nothing.

A screenshot of every clever hedge fund and alt ETF, scored against just buying the index. The scoreboard is brutal.

A screenshot made the rounds this week, and it is the most honest thing I have seen on finance Twitter in a while. Someone pulled the lifetime stats for about 53 of the fanciest funds money can buy and lined them up against one thing: just owning the S&P. Long/short equity, market neutral, managed futures, merger arbitrage, the full menu of sophisticated products with the fees to match.

Every one of these funds is sold on the same promise. A smoother ride, and protection when the market finally breaks. Returns that do not lean entirely on stocks going up. That is the pitch you pay up for.

Here is the receipt.

Look at the information ratio column, and most of it is bleeding red.

The information ratio measures how much extra return a fund earned for the tracking risk it took against the benchmark. Positive means you were rewarded for stepping away from the index. Negative means the index would have paid you more for less. More than 40 of the 53 names on this list are negative. For most of these funds, a plain SPY position over the same years handed you more return per unit of risk, with none of the fees and none of the lockups.

The ones that look elite fall apart the second you check the date column.

The top of the table is seductive. WTLS at a Sharpe of 2.64. MEMA at 2.10. EMEQ at 2.08. Numbers a real hedge fund would frame on the wall. Then you look one column left: WTLS has 92 days of history. MEMA has 115. That is three or four months. You can post a Sharpe of 2.64 over a single quarter by being long the right thing at the right time and calling it skill. It is recency wearing a lab coat.

Now read the funds that carry a real track record, the ones with the full 1610 trading days since the end of 2019. They cluster around a Sharpe of 0.6 to 1.1. Respectable, and still below what the index itself did over the same stretch, while charging you like a hedge fund to trail it.

The crash-insurance trap

Most of these products are sold as insurance. You give up some upside in the good years so you are covered when the market falls apart. The two columns that test that promise are up capture and down capture: how much of the market's gains you keep, and how much of its losses you eat.

A good hedge has low down capture without killing its up capture. That balance is the entire job.

Almost none of them pull it off. The dedicated short and anti-beta funds are the cautionary tale. BTAL, built to rise when the market falls, posted a CAGR of -0.08 over the period. It did its one job on the worst days and bled capital every other day for six years. JPLS sits at the very bottom of the page with a Sharpe of -3.86 and a CAGR of -0.42. That is a product that was harmful to own.

Low down capture is worthless if your up capture is crushed, because most years are up years.

The fair caveat

I want to be straight about the test itself, because the funds deserve it. The window here, end of 2019 through mid 2026, contains one of the great equity runs in history. That is the hardest possible environment for anything designed to hedge. Score crash insurance during a six-year melt-up and of course it looks terrible.

So the lesson is not that alternatives are a scam. Plenty of these strategies will earn their keep in a decade that finally has a bad stretch. The lesson is the bar. If you are going to pay alt-fund fees for diversification, the thing has to survive the up years well enough that you still own it when the down year arrives. The single cleanest outperformer on the whole list, SPMO, is an S&P 500 momentum ETF. It barely hedges anything. It owns the index with a tilt and charges almost nothing.

Why I am showing you this

I am putting those same two columns, up capture and down capture, on my own published track record starting now. Every idea I post gets graded on how much of the market's upside it caught and how much of the downside it ate, against SPY, in plain sight.

If a strategy only knows how to win when the market wins, it is beta in a costume. The table going around this week is a room full of expensive costumes. The least I can do is hold my own work to the same standard I just used to judge theirs.

Stats read from the lifetime table circulating this week (2019-12-31 to 2026-06-04, versus SPY). The up and down capture lens I am adding to my track record is computed the same way: my equity curve against SPY, on the days the index rose and the days it fell. As always, this is how I am thinking, not what you should do.

You're Being Paid 0.3% to Take the Risk

The S&P just printed new highs. Here is the receipt for the next decade — and it's almost blank.

Pull up any timeline today and the mood is the same: new highs, "stay long," the only mistake is not being aggressive enough. Maybe. But there's a number nobody at the top wants to look at, and I ran it this week so you don't have to take my word for it.

It's the equity risk premium — the extra return you expect for owning stocks instead of just parking in Treasuries. Historically that reward runs 3 to 4 percent a year. Right now, on the actual building blocks, it is +0.3%.

Three-tenths of one percent. That's what the market is paying you, per year, to ride the rollercoaster instead of clipping a T-bill and going back to bed.

The receipt

Forget forecasts and vibes. The long-run return of an asset isn't a mystery — it's mostly arithmetic you can write down today. For stocks, the cleanest anchor is the cyclically-adjusted earnings yield: one divided by the CAPE ratio. The CAPE sits at 42.7 — roughly twice its 50-year average. Flip it over and you get the real return the market is offering from here.

Here is what the whole menu looks like, in real terms (after inflation), per year:


Read it twice. Investment-grade corporate bonds — the boring stuff your dentist owns — are priced to return more than the S&P over the next decade, with a fraction of the heartburn. Stocks at +2.3% real sit a rounding error above the 10-year Treasury at +2.1%. That gap, the +0.3%, is the premium. It's the thinnest I've measured.

And that's the optimistic read, the one that assumes valuations never normalize. If the CAPE drifts even halfway back toward its historical range over the next fifteen years, that repricing is a headwind of roughly four points a year. Run the building blocks with that assumption and the expected real return on US stocks goes negative. Not a crash call. Just gravity.

"But I'll hide in quality, right?"

Here's where it gets interesting, because this is the move everyone reaches for when they finally admit valuations are stretched: rotate into the safe, low-volatility, high-quality names and let the casino names blow up. It's the textbook defensive trade. Betting-against-beta. The low-vol anomaly. Decades of academic papers say boring wins on a risk-adjusted basis.

So I tested it on our own universe — about a thousand liquid US stocks, 2022 through today. Long the calmest, lowest-beta names; short the wildest. Clean, cost-aware, out-of-sample.

It lost. Badly. The low-risk basket underperformed the high-risk basket in every slice — the boring names didn't protect you, they just left money on the table while the high-beta AI complex ran. The "hide in quality" reflex, the one that's supposed to be the smart-money escape hatch, has been the wrong trade for this entire cycle.

I'm not telling you that to sell you the opposite. I'm telling you because it's the honest shape of the problem: the expected return is low and the usual defensive playbook hasn't worked. There's no clean place to hide and no obvious lever to pull harder.

So what do you actually do

When the market offers the least, the instinct is to swing harder — concentrate into the seven names that have worked, lever up the winners, squeeze the last drop out of the trend. And it keeps working, right up until the afternoon it doesn't.

Antti Ilmanen, who wrote the book this whole exercise comes from, has a quieter answer, and it's aged better than any price target: when expected returns are low, stop trying to manufacture returns you can't control and start compounding the edges you can.

You don't control the CAPE. You don't control whether the AI trade runs another year. What you control is the stuff that quietly decides where you finish:

  • Diversify your sources of return, not just your tickers. Ilmanen calls genuine diversification the only free lunch in finance. A book that only knows how to win when momentum wins is one regime change from a bad decade.
  • Mind the costs and the taxes. At a +2.3% real expected return, a 1% fee isn't a rounding error — it's a third of your edge.
  • Size like the next decade is hard, because the math says it is. The investors who survive low-return regimes aren't the ones who called the top. They're the ones who never had to be right about it.

The high is loud. The receipt is quiet. +0.3% is what you're being paid to take the risk — so make sure the parts you actually control are doing the heavy lifting, because the market isn't going to.
