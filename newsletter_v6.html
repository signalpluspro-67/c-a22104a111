The S&P closed the week at 746 on the SPY, up about a percent on the week and four and a half percent over the trailing month. On the surface, healthy. The cap-weighted indices you see quoted on television are being levitated by a small group of giant companies while the body of the market quietly treads water.

I want to be scrupulously fair about what that does and does not mean, because I have been burned before by treating narrowness as a sell signal. A narrow market is a fragile market, yes. But fragile is not the same as falling. Sometimes a narrow tape is the early innings of a genuine new leadership regime — think of the first eighteen months of the internet build-out, or the first leg of the post-2023 AI trade — and fighting that narrowness because it offends your sense of "healthy breadth" is one of the most expensive mistakes in the book. The question this letter exists to answer is not "is the market narrow." We know it is narrow. The question is whether the narrowing is the warning or the trade, and that is a genuinely two-sided question that I will not pretend to resolve with a slogan.

To get the most out of these recaps, and to understand the framework behind every observation here, I encourage you to read about my methodology. Now let us go layer by layer.

Market Health

the index is fine, the field is asleep

The first thing I do every weekend, before I look at a single index level, is count the field. Not the index — the field. The distinction is everything. The S&P 500 you see quoted is a capitalization-weighted average, which means a handful of trillion-dollar companies carry vastly more weight than the hundreds of ordinary names beneath them. That index can keep printing green long after the typical stock has rolled over and started bleeding, simply because the giants are still rising. If you only watch the index, you will be the last person in the room to notice the floor has gone soft. So I look at the median stock, and I look at participation, and those two readings this week tell a story the index level will never tell you.

The single most revealing number in this entire letter is this one: the median 14-day RSI across the full universe I track closed the week at 50.3. Fifty. Dead, flat, perfectly neutral center. Now I want you to actually sit with how strange that is. The Nasdaq is up eight percent in a month. The S&P is at all-time highs. By every headline you would think the market is in a confident, broad advance. And yet the momentum reading of the typical stock says it has gone precisely nowhere. Not up. Not down. Asleep.

The distribution confirms it. Only 2.8% of the stocks I track register as overbought; only 2.6% register as oversold. In a genuinely broad, healthy bull market, that overbought figure swells — you routinely see fifteen, twenty, twenty-five percent of stocks pushed into overbought territory because almost everything is participating in the advance. That is what strength looks like from the inside: a wide, deep, democratic rally where the median stock is being dragged higher along with the leaders. What we have instead is a distribution pinned flat in the middle, with neither tail stretched. The enthusiasm that exists so visibly on the index screen simply does not exist in the body of the market.

Only the latter is a top in progress. We have the fragility. We do not yet have the breakdown.

Now here is where I force myself to argue against my own thesis, because this is the part most letters skip and it is the part that actually matters. Narrowness is not a timing signal, and I have the work to prove it to myself. When I backtest the equal-weight-to-cap-weight relationship — the RSP/SPY ratio that every bear on financial television points to as the canary in the coal mine — the result is genuinely uncomfortable for the bear case. Over the last several years, mega-cap concentration has been bullish on a one-to-two-month forward horizon, not bearish. The sign comes out inverted from the intuition, and it comes out with strong statistical significance — this is not a marginal, noisy finding. In plain English: betting against this rally simply because it is narrow has been a reliable way to lose money. The concentration persists. The concentration pays. The giants keep winning because money flows to liquidity and momentum, and liquidity and momentum live in the giants. Right up until the moment they don't.

So I hold two ideas in my head at the same time, which is the only honest way to hold this market. Idea one: the field is asleep, and a sleeping field is a fragility, a market resting its entire weight on a few load-bearing columns. Idea two: that fragility has, historically and statistically, been more profitable to lean with than against, until a specific confirming crack appears. And I want to be precise about what that crack is, because "low breadth" is not it — we already have low breadth and it has not mattered for weeks. The crack I am waiting for is narrower and more specific: breadth divergence at a new high that the mega-caps then fail to defend. Index at new highs, internals deteriorating, and the leadership itself stumbling. That specific three-part configuration is the one that, in the historical record, has preceded mild but real index underperformance with statistical weight behind it — the kind of topping divergence that showed up before the 2021-2022 turn and in the late innings of prior concentration cycles. We are sitting in the setup. We do not yet have the trigger. The leaders have not stumbled. And the difference between "the setup is present" and "the trigger has fired" is the difference between being positioned and being merely early — a distinction I would rather state honestly than paper over to make my caution look smarter than it is.

One last note for this section, and it is a historical one, because context is the antidote to panic. We have lived through narrow markets before and they did not all end the same way. The late-1990s tape narrowed for the better part of two years before it broke — the generals marched while the soldiers deserted, and the people who shorted the narrowness in 1998 were carried out long before the actual top in 2000. Conversely, the 2007 narrowing resolved much faster and more violently. The lesson is not "narrow means crash." The lesson is "narrow means fragile, and fragile markets break on a catalyst, not on a calendar." Which is precisely why the rest of this letter is a hunt for the catalyst, not a countdown on a clock.

Volatility

the market is hedging concentration, not catastrophe

The volatility complex this week tells one clean, specific story, and I want to be precise about what it is and — just as important — what it is not. It is not pricing a macro tail. There is no broad crash premium being bid across the whole market. What it is pricing, and pricing with surgical precision, is the exact concentration we just walked through. The hedging is not a blanket. It is a scalpel, and it is aimed at the precise handful of names carrying the index.

Start with the headline that everyone quotes and almost everyone misreads. The VIX closed the week at 16.70, down sharply over the trailing month. On its face, calm. Sixteen and a half is not a number that screams fear; it is a number that whispers complacency. And the whole front of the volatility curve is in clean contango — short-dated volatility cheaper than long-dated, which is the normal, healthy, boring state of the world. In calm times nobody pays up for protection they might need tomorrow, so the near-term contracts trade at a discount to the far ones.

That 1.199 ratio matters more than the absolute VIX level, and it is one of my favorite tells for how close we actually are to a real top, so let me explain the mechanism properly. In a calm market, three-month implied volatility trades at a healthy premium to spot — here, about twenty percent richer. As genuine fear enters the market, that relationship compresses: traders rush to buy front-month protection faster than they buy long-dated, because the thing they are afraid of is now, not in six months. The ratio falls toward 1.00. In a true panic it inverts below 1.00 into outright backwardation — front-month vol more expensive than long-dated — which is the unmistakable signature of a market in a real fear spiral, the configuration we saw for a few hours during the August 2024 carry unwind and during the worst of 2022. At 1.20, we are not close to that. The hedging market, for now, is pricing an ordinary pullback, not a crisis. I keep saying for now on purpose, because these relationships turn in days, not weeks — but I will not pretend the front of the curve is screaming when it is, at most, clearing its throat.

Now the two ratios that are firing, because this is where the concentration finally shows its face in the options market. The first is VVIX. The VVIX measures the volatility of the VIX itself — the fear inside the fear gauge, the price of options on volatility. It closed at 91, and against a spot VIX of 16.7 that puts the VVIX/VIX ratio up around 5.46, which is elevated. When VVIX runs hot relative to VIX, it tells you someone is paying a meaningful premium to protect against a sharp, sudden move higher in volatility — not a slow grind up in fear, but a violent gap. That is precisely the protection a large institution buys when it is sitting on a concentrated, profitable book and is worried not about a drift lower but about waking up to a gap-down. It is gap insurance, and gap insurance gets bid when the people holding the most have the most to lose in a single bad morning.

The second ratio is the one that ties the entire section to everything above it, and it is the VXN/VIX ratio. The VXN is the volatility index for the Nasdaq-100 specifically — tech's own fear gauge. It closed at 22.8, which against the 16.7 broad-market VIX puts the ratio at 1.37. Tech-specific implied volatility is running thirty-seven percent hot relative to the broad market. Now read that against the entire Market Health section and it locks into place with a satisfying, slightly ominous click: the vol market is not hedging the S&P. It is hedging the Nasdaq. It is paying up specifically for protection on the exact mega-cap technology complex that is doing all of the index lifting. The concentration in the rally and the concentration in the hedging are the same five or six names. The cash market is buying them and the options market is insuring them, and both markets are telling you, independently, exactly where the load-bearing columns are. When the options market is willing to pay a thirty-seven percent premium to insure one corner of the market, it is telling you where it thinks the fragility lives. It lives in tech.

The SKEW index measures the cost of deep out-of-the-money puts — the institutional crash hedge, the insurance against a once-a-decade collapse — relative to ordinary at-the-money puts. It closed at 137. That is present, but it is not extreme. So the deep-tail crash bid has actually cooled from where it was in mid-May. Read that carefully, because it cuts against my own bias: institutions are not currently piling into catastrophe protection. They are buying targeted, mega-cap-tech, sharp-move-higher-in-vol insurance. That is a concentration hedge, not a catastrophe hedge, and the distinction is the difference between a market positioning for a pullback and a market positioning for a crash. None of the three has flipped. If two of the three move together in the same week, the regime changes character — from hedging concentration to hedging contagion — and that is the moment I stop writing the word "pullback" and start writing something larger. We are not there. But that triad is the dashboard, and I would rather hand you the dashboard than hand you my conclusion.

Pairs Alignment

every relationship is telling the same story

I love pairs because they are honest in a way a single index can never be. An index level can lie to you — it can be carried, dragged, distorted by a handful of components. But a relationship between two indices cancels out the common factor and leaves only the divergence, the pure signal of where money is actually flowing relative to where it is not. And this week, every single pair I follow is telling the same story from a different window. When four independent relationships all point in the same direction, you stop calling it coincidence and start calling it the regime.

QQQ versus equal-weight (RSP and QQQE). This is the purest concentration read that exists. Strip the mega-caps of their weight, count every company the same, and the Nasdaq's eight-percent month collapses to two. The cap-weighted-to-equal-weight spread is stretched about as wide as it gets outside of a genuine mania — and notice, this is the same dispersion the volatility market is pricing through VXN, just expressed in cash prices instead of implied vol. Think about how powerful that cross-confirmation is. Two completely different instruments — the options market and the cash equity market — operated by completely different participants with completely different incentives, are independently measuring the identical thing: this rally is five names deep, not five hundred. When two unrelated witnesses tell you the same story, you believe the story.

QQQ versus IWM. Technology is outperforming small caps for what is now the better part of two months, and the persistence is the tell. Small caps are the most rate-sensitive, most domestically-geared, most "is the actual real economy fine" corner of the entire market. When they lag this relentlessly during a period when the VIX is falling — when fear is draining out of the market — it tells you the soft-landing trade, the "everything is fine, the economy is strong, rates will come down gently" trade, is not being bought broadly. It is being expressed in mega-cap technology and almost nowhere else. The bid is narrow because the conviction is narrow. People are not buying "the economy." They are buying eight specific companies.

SPY versus the dollar (UUP and DXY). Stocks are rising while the dollar holds firm — the dollar index sits near 99.3 and has been firm over the month. A strong dollar alongside strong stocks is, more often than not, the fingerprint of foreign capital flowing into the largest, most liquid US names. Overseas institutional money does not buy the Russell 2000; it cannot, the liquidity is not deep enough to absorb it. It buys the Magnificent names, in size, through the most liquid vehicles on earth. So the strong-dollar-strong-stocks combination is itself, quietly, a third concentration signal: the marginal global buyer is reaching for the exact same eight stocks everyone else already owns, because those are the only names that can absorb the size.

Stocks versus bonds. This is the one that has nagged at me for weeks, and it has not resolved — if anything it has deepened. Bonds are refusing to follow equities higher. The long end has been heavy; the twenty-year Treasury proxy is down on the month, and the bond market has spent weeks pricing a materially different path than the stock market is pricing. The Fed does not meet until the middle of June, but the bond market is not waiting for permission to have an opinion. It has a view, it has held that view with conviction for weeks, and that view is decidedly not the cheerful one equities are expressing. When two enormous, deep, liquid markets disagree this persistently about the same future, one of them is wrong — and across my career, when the stock market and the bond market disagree, the bond market is the one I have learned to bet on being right. Bonds are run by people whose entire job is to be paranoid about the future. Stocks are run by people whose job is to be optimistic about it. In a standoff, paranoia has the better track record.

The market that actually exists, beneath the comforting index level, is a narrow, mega-cap, foreign-and-systematically-bid, technology-led tape, and the bond market does not believe the optimism underneath it. I want to be careful with my language here: that is not a bearish forecast. It is a description of the present. The forecast comes later, in My Take, where I will own my lean. But the description is not a matter of opinion — it is what the relationships say, and they say it without my help.

The Energy line deserves its own paragraph, because it connects to a macro theme I have been tracking for a month. Energy is the only non-technology sector with any genuine pulse, and crude has been firm — the oil complex is up nearly eight percent over the trailing month. In past letters I have flagged that energy strength during a period of broad-market stalling has historically been a regime tell, and I want to explain the mechanism rather than just assert the pattern. When oil pulls up while the rest of the market stalls, it is frequently the market quietly beginning to price an inflation reset — a recognition that the disinflation everyone has been counting on to deliver rate cuts may be stalling out. And rising energy costs are a tax on every other sector's margins. Historically, when energy outperforms a flat-to-down broad tape, the broad tape has tended to follow lower within a couple of quarters, because the same inflationary impulse that lifts oil eventually pressures the rate path that the rest of the market depends on. I do not want to over-fit this narrative — there are Iran headlines and OPEC supply dynamics doing some of the lifting, and geopolitics is not a clean signal. But the flow is the flow, and energy leadership in a stalling market is not a comfortable historical companion.

The detail I always watch inside the sector tape is the Discretionary-versus-Staples relationship, because it is the single cleanest read on whether the consumer-facing economy is leaning offensive or defensive. The logic is simple and durable: when investors are confident, they buy Consumer Discretionary — the cyclical, the non-essential, the aspirational, the stuff people buy when they feel rich. When they are nervous, they rotate into Consumer Staples — the toothpaste and soup and laundry detergent that people buy in any economy. Right now, both are barely positive and both are lagging the index, jammed together near the bottom of the pack. In a confident expansion, Discretionary pulls away from Staples by a wide and widening margin. We do not have that. We have both sitting flat and lagging in a dead heat, which says the consumer trade is neither offensive nor defensive — it is simply absent, ceding the entire stage to mega-cap technology. And that is the deepest read of this whole section: when the only genuine conviction in the entire eleven-sector map lives in a single sector, the market does not have breadth. It has one enormous bet wearing the costume of a diversified index.

FX

159 — one candle from the line that matters

I have said for weeks that the single most important chart in the world right now is not an equity chart at all. It is USDJPY. And this week it closed at 159.16, which means we are sitting one good candle away from 160 — the line I have circled in red on my screen for a month, the line I genuinely lose sleep over.

Let me re-explain why this level is not casual, because it is the single biggest non-obvious risk in the entire macro picture and most retail investors do not watch it at all. The Japanese yen has been weakening relentlessly for one reason above all others: the carry trade is alive, enormous, and self-reinforcing. The mechanic is simple enough to explain in a sentence and consequential enough to move the whole world. You borrow yen at near-zero Japanese interest rates. You convert those borrowed yen into dollars. You buy higher-yielding US assets with the proceeds — and increasingly, you buy US mega-cap technology specifically, because that is where the momentum and the liquidity are. As long as the Bank of Japan stays quiet and the yen keeps drifting weaker, this trade prints money on two fronts at once: you earn the yield differential and you earn the currency move as the yen falls against your dollar assets. So the flow keeps feeding the exact same handful of names that are levitating the index. I want you to internalize this because it is the hidden hinge of the entire market: the carry trade and the equity concentration are not two separate stories. They are the same story. The cheap-yen funding is part of the engine buying the mega-caps. The narrow rally is, in part, a leveraged yen-funded rally.

Now the danger, and the reason 159 keeps me up. The 160 zone is where the Japanese Ministry of Finance has historically turned from "watching the markets closely" to "ready to act." It is the level they defended with documented, confirmed intervention in 2024, spending tens of billions of dollars to arrest the yen's slide. So 159 is not just a number on a screen — it is the literal doorstep of the level where Japanese authorities have stepped in before, and where the BoJ's rhetoric reliably starts to sharpen. The broad dollar is firm, with the dollar index near 99.3, which is entirely consistent with the strong-dollar-strong-stocks foreign-bid picture from the pairs section. Everything is connected. The firm dollar, the weak yen, the crowded mega-caps — they are facets of one trade.

Here is the asymmetry that defines my caution, and I want to lay out both branches honestly. If USDJPY simply pushes through 160 with the Ministry of Finance choosing not to act, the carry trade rebuilds, the yen stops being the immediate pivot, and the mega-cap bid gets another tailwind — which is, in the short run, bullish for exactly the names already carrying everything. That is the branch my bias does not want to think about, and it is a real branch. But the other branch is the one that matters more, because of its violence. If 160 holds and the yen reverses hard — whether through actual intervention, through intervention rhetoric alone, or through a simple positioning unwind as the trade gets too crowded — then somewhere through roughly 155 the entire thing changes character. A strengthening yen forces carry unwinds, and the mechanism is brutal and self-reinforcing: funds that borrowed cheap yen to buy US assets now have to sell those US assets to repay loans that are suddenly more expensive in dollar terms. And which assets do they sell? The ones they bought. The mega-cap technology complex. The unwind risk sits on the exact same shoulders as the rally.

We have a live, recent rehearsal for exactly this, and it is why I take it so seriously. In early August 2024, a modest BoJ hike combined with a crowded carry position produced one of the most violent short-lived equity dislocations in years — a global vol spike, a several-percent gap lower in US equities, and a VIX that briefly exploded into the high 30s and 40s intraday before the unwind exhausted itself. It resolved quickly, but it was a clean demonstration of the physics: when the yen-funded carry unwinds, the names that benefited most from it get hit first and hardest, and because in 2026 those names are the names carrying the index, the index goes with them. The Lottery

a tail ticket, sized like a tail ticket

A word of process honesty before I give you anything, because I would rather lose your interest than your trust. Given everything in this letter — the concentration, the carry trade pinned at 159, the bond market's quiet and deepening warning — the lottery that actually fits the thesis is not a long at all. It is a tail hedge. A small, strictly defined-risk position in volatility or downside protection that costs a little and pays a lot if the cluster I described in My Take fires: USDJPY rejects 160, the AI names stumble on next week's earnings, PCE runs hot, and the narrow tape finally cracks the way I think it eventually does. The structure is long-dated VIX calls or out-of-the-money SPY puts, dated past the June FOMC so that the single biggest scheduled catalyst sits inside the life of the position. This is not a prediction that any of it happens on a timetable. It is a cheap ticket that converts my caution from a feeling into a position — something that pays if my thesis is right and costs me a rounding error if it is wrong. It is, in a real sense, the trade that lets me stay patient without feeling like I am doing nothing.

The discipline on a lottery is the discipline that matters most, because lotteries are where undisciplined traders quietly bleed out, so let me state the rules exactly as I follow them. First: no initial stop. A lottery is defined-risk by construction — the premium I pay is the risk, the entire amount I can lose — so I size it from the start so that a total loss is a number I will genuinely not feel. There is nothing to stop out of; the position was already a known, bounded cost the moment I put it on. Second: tiny size. Half a percent to one percent of book, no more, ever. A lottery that is sized like a real position is not a lottery, it is a mistake. Third — and this is where almost everyone gives it all back — if the thing actually runs, I take profits in tranches as it multiplies rather than holding for the dream of the perfect exit. A tail hedge that triples and then round-trips back to zero is a worse outcome than one I trimmed methodically on the way up, because the first one taught me to hold and punished me for it. Size it small, define the risk as the premium, scale out on the way up. That is the entire game, and the people who lose at it are almost always the people who skipped one of those three rules.

The Philosopher's Corner

on being early

I want to close, as I have been doing, with a thought that is less about the tape and more about the temperament it takes to trade the tape well — because in the end the edge is not in the screeners, it is in the psychology that lets you actually follow them. This week's thought is about being early, because being early is the precise thing I am living right now, and I suspect some of you are living it with me.

There is a particular and exquisite kind of pain that markets reserve for the person who is right too soon. You see the fragility clearly. You count the field and watch the median stock sleep at 50. You note the yen pinned at the line, the bond market refusing to confirm, the vol market quietly insuring the exact names doing all the lifting. And you know — with the quiet, grounded certainty of someone who has actually done the work rather than just felt a vibe — that this cannot hold forever. And then it holds. It holds for another week. Another month. And every single day it holds, the market leans in and whispers that you were wrong, that the people who ignored the fragility and stayed fully long were the smart ones all along, that your caution was never discipline at all but cowardice wearing discipline's clothes. The market is a genius at this particular cruelty. It does not just take your money when you are wrong; it takes your conviction when you are right but early, which is worse, because conviction is the only thing that lets you act when the moment finally comes.

The philosopher's answer — the thing I hold onto in exactly these weeks — is to ruthlessly separate two things the market deliberately conflates: being correct and being vindicated. They are not the same thing, and the market pays them out on completely different and unrelated schedules. You can be correct for a very long time before you are vindicated. The entire psychological game of this profession, the part nobody puts in the marketing, is surviving — financially and emotionally — the gap between the two. And cash, in that gap, is not a position of weakness or indecision. Cash is the physical, balance-sheet form of patience. It is the refusal to let the market's timing dictate your conviction. It is the discipline made tangible. When I hold the heaviest cash position of my year, I am not sitting on my hands; I am holding a loaded position whose ammunition is patience and whose target is the pullback that lets me buy quality on sale while everyone else is forced to sell it in panic.

The Stoics had a phrase for this that I keep close, closer in weeks like this one: focus only on what is within your control. I cannot control when the pullback comes. I genuinely cannot control whether the yen breaks 160 next Tuesday or next quarter, or whether PCE runs hot or cool, or whether the AI names beat or miss. None of that is mine to command. What is mine to command — entirely, completely — is whether I am positioned to act with clarity and calm when the moment finally arrives. Whether I have the cash. Whether I have done the homework and built the shopping list. Whether I have the emotional composure to buy quality on sale while the tape is screaming and the crowd is selling. Being early is only a mistake if it makes you abandon your discipline before the thesis has a chance to play out — if the loneliness of being right-but-not-yet-paid pushes you to capitulate and chase the very rally you distrusted, right at the top. Held correctly, with the temperament intact, being early is simply patience that has not yet been paid. And patience, unlike a stock, does not expire.

I would rather be early and liquid than late and trapped. Ask me again in a month whether that was wisdom or stubbornness, and I will tell you honestly which one it turned out to be. But either way — and this is the whole point — the cash will still be there, and so will I, ready to act. That readiness is the only edge that survives every regime.
