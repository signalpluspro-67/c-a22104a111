The 10-year yield: the biggest single-day move in two months

The ten-year Treasury yield closed Friday at 4.595 percent, which is +3.00 percent higher than its Thursday close at 4.461 percent, and that one-day move is the largest absolute single-day percentage change in the 10-year yield over the past sixty trading days. The next biggest move in that sixty-day window was on March 20, at +2.57 percent, and the third biggest was on March 30, at -2.21 percent. So the Friday move is not just inside the top five, it is the single largest move, by a meaningful margin, in the most recent sixty trading sessions. On the week the 10-year added +5.29 percent, which means the marginal buyer of duration disappeared not over a slow grind but in a concentrated late-week event. The 30-year, at the closing yield of 5.128 percent, climbed +2.31 percent Friday and +3.66 percent on the week, which is a smaller move in percentage terms but a similar story in direction. The five-year, at 4.258 percent, climbed +3.32 percent Friday and +6.11 percent on the week, and that's the curve that matters most for the marginal cost of risk capital in equities. The entire belly of the curve repriced upward on the same Friday session.

What makes a single-day move in yields a regime signal rather than noise is the question of what was the marginal seller. In a Fed cut cycle, big single-day moves in yields tend to be Fed surprise events. In a no-Fed week, big single-day moves in yields tend to be systematic deleveraging out of long-duration positioning. Friday May 15 was a no-Fed week. There was no auction surprise, no Powell speech, no CPI miss, no Treasury announcement. The move came from positioning. The question of who was forced to sell and why is the same question as the question of who is going to be forced to sell next. And the answer is mechanical: when the marginal funding rate moves three percent in a session, every levered carry book in the world has to recompute its risk and decide whether it can hold the same duration through next week. Many of them cannot.

The second-order effect is what makes this matter for equities. The long-duration end of the equity market, which is tech and growth, prices off the long end of the Treasury curve through the discounted cash flow mechanism. A +3 percent move in the 10-year is mathematically a meaningful change in the discount rate applied to cash flows ten, fifteen, twenty years out. The reason this didn't immediately show up in XLK is that XLK is dominated by eight names whose own price action mechanically holds the sector up regardless of what the rates side says. We will come back to this in Section Two, because it is the central mechanism that lets the index print an ATH while the curve sends a different message.

Small caps: twice the magnitude, in the opposite direction

The Russell 2000 ETF, IWM, closed Friday at 277.60, down -2.41 percent on the session, which is exactly twice the magnitude of the SPY move at -1.20 percent. On the week IWM was down -2.31 percent, while SPY was up +0.21 percent, which is a weekly spread of -2.52 percentage points in the small-cap direction. The midcaps, MDY, were down -2.36 percent on the week. Equal-weight SPY, the RSP ETF, was down -1.24 percent on the week. Russell 1000 Value, IWN, was down -2.67 percent. The pattern is consistent: every measure of the market that is not dominated by the eight mega-caps was down on the week, by a meaningful amount, while the headline index was up.

In a healthy correction, small caps and large caps move in the same order of magnitude. The differential is usually one to one and a half. When small caps move at two times the magnitude of large caps to the downside, the signal is that the marginal allocator is reducing risk by selling the most fragile end of the curve first. That is the textbook beta liquidation pattern. It is what you see in late-1999, in late-2007, in February 2020. It is not what you see in mid-uptrend pullbacks. Mid-uptrend pullbacks have IWM down -1.2 percent when SPY is down -1.2 percent, not IWM down -2.4 percent when SPY is down -1.2 percent. The ratio is the tell.

The structural reason the ratio is the tell is that small caps are roughly twice as rate-sensitive as the index on a duration basis, because their cash flow profile is more weighted toward the next five years than the next fifteen, because their funding cost is more variable, and because they hold meaningfully more variable-rate debt. So when the 10-year moves three percent in a day, small caps mechanically reprice harder than the index. The fact that the move was clean and concentrated on Friday, rather than spread across the week, reinforces the rates-driven interpretation. If the small-cap weakness had been a slow grind starting Monday, you could blame earnings dispersion or sector rotation. The fact that it was a Friday cliff matches the yield move directly. Same day, same mechanism.

SKEW: 145.77, the 80th percentile since 2016

The SKEW index closed Friday at 145.77, up +4.63 percent on the session and +5.47 percent on the week. SKEW measures the relative cost of very far out of the money S&P 500 puts against standard at-the-money puts. The way SKEW is constructed, a reading at 100 means tail risk is priced symmetrically with body risk. A reading at 145 means the market is paying a substantial premium for crash-tail protection beyond what it pays for ordinary downside protection. The historical distribution since 2016 makes the 145.77 reading sit at the 79.9th percentile, which is to say the top 20.1 percent of all readings in nearly a decade.

A SKEW reading in the top decile is not, by itself, a reliable timing signal. SKEW can sit elevated for weeks while the index drifts higher. What matters about SKEW is the rate of change combined with the level, and what matters about Friday is that SKEW jumped while the session was selling off. That is the wrong direction for the standard hedging cycle. The standard hedging cycle has institutions buying SKEW protection before sessions sell off, and selling it once the sell-off is in progress to lock in the gain on the hedge. When SKEW rises into a down session, the interpretation is that the institutions buying tail protection on Friday were not the ones who already had it. They were buying because they were under-hedged for the move that just started, and they were rushing to catch up.

That is the signature of a hedging panic, on a small scale, inside an otherwise calm market. The VIX itself, at 18.43, is not a panic reading. The VIX9D, at 16.37, is also not panic. But the VIX9D rose +8.12 percent on Friday alone and +15.20 percent on the week, which is the kind of short-dated curve move that says the next two weeks are being priced for more volatility than the next ninety days. The VIX3M, at 21.36, is still well above spot, which means the curve has not flipped to backwardation, but the front of the curve is starting to catch up to the back, and the ratio VIX3M over VIX is now at 1.159, down from 1.21 a week ago. Curve compression is the second tell that pairs with elevated SKEW. When both happen together, institutional hedging is restructuring from passive long-dated to active short-dated, which is exactly what you see ahead of binary events the market thinks could break the regime.

The binary event the market is pricing is Nvidia on Wednesday May 20, which we will get to in Section Three. For now, the data point I want you to remember is that SKEW at 145.77 plus VIX3M over VIX at 1.159 has occurred together fewer than thirty times in the last decade, and most of those occurrences clustered around inflection points rather than around mid-trend pullbacks. The conditional behavior of the index over the sixty days following such joint readings is not the same as the unconditional drift.

Why five simultaneous matters more than any one

There is a temptation, when you read a passage like the one I just wrote, to dismiss the five internals individually. Maybe the yield move was a positioning quirk. Maybe small caps were weak because of two specific bank stocks. Maybe gold was just consolidating after a long run. Maybe SKEW always sits high in May. Each of these alternative explanations is, in isolation, plausible. None of them survive the requirement that all five be simultaneously true on the same Friday session.

The math of independent observations is unforgiving. If each of the five internals had, conditional on a non-regime-change day, a 20 percent probability of producing a top-decile reading by chance, the joint probability of all five doing so on the same session would be 0.20 to the fifth power, which is 0.00032, or roughly three in ten thousand. That is a rough back-of-the-envelope calculation that assumes independence, and the internals are not independent, but the spirit of the calculation holds. Five lectures aligned on the same Friday is a regime signal, not noise. The Bayesian update on the probability of the next sixty trading days containing a meaningful drawdown is non-trivial.

In the data I keep, the joint condition of (TNX one-day move at +3 percent or more) and (SKEW above 140) and (IWM weekly underperformance against SPY of more than 2 percentage points) and (KRE weekly decline of more than 3 percent) has occurred only a handful of times in the past decade. The conditional sixty-day SPY return following such joint events is meaningfully negative compared to the unconditional baseline. I am not going to put a single number on it because the sample size is too small to publish with confidence, but the direction is clear and the magnitude is large enough to matter for sizing decisions.

That is the first reason I am almost entirely in cash. That is the first reason I am not chasing this rally. The internals already told me what Wednesday will probably confirm.

The historical precedents that match

I want to be careful with historical analogies, because every late-cycle thesis I have ever read leans on them and most of those analogies are weak. Markets do not actually repeat. They rhyme inconsistently. But there is a class of joint conditions that does, in fact, recur with some reliability, and the joint condition I described above (yield spike + small-cap underperformance + regional bank weakness + gold weakness + SKEW elevation, all on the same week) is in that class. The three episodes that match closest, in the past fifteen years, are the second half of 2007, the spring of 2018, and the months immediately preceding February 2020.

The 2007 episode is the cleanest analog. From late July 2007 through October 2007, the S&P 500 reached its all-time high on October 9 at 1565, while underneath the surface the regional bank index was already down meaningfully from its earlier 2007 high, the BBB credit spread had widened by roughly 70 basis points, the 10-year yield had completed a sharp move from 5.30 percent in June down to 4.10 percent in September, which is the opposite direction from today but the same internal dispersion pattern, and the small-cap index had underperformed the large-cap index by approximately 8 percentage points over the prior three months. The index made its October 9 high on the back of mega-cap leadership (AAPL, GOOG, RIMM, BIDU at the time), and the entire structure cracked over the following nineteen weeks, with the S&P 500 down 20 percent by mid-March 2008 and the regional bank index down 40 percent. The internal signal preceded the surface signal by roughly three months, and the trade was visible in real time to anyone watching the regional bank tape.

The 2018 episode is messier and less complete. From late August through early October 2018, the S&P 500 reached its then-all-time-high at 2940 on September 20, while the small-cap index had been distributing for nearly two months. The 10-year yield rose sharply from 2.85 percent to 3.25 percent over that window, exactly the kind of rates-led repricing we see today, and the regional bank index broke its tape in the first week of October. The full correction unfolded over twelve weeks, with the S&P 500 down 20 percent by December 24, 2018. The internal signal preceded the headline by roughly four weeks, and the rates-led mechanism was clearly identifiable from the curve action.

The February 2020 episode is the one most people remember, but the structural lead time was actually shorter than the previous two. The S&P 500 made its pre-COVID high at 3393 on February 19, 2020, with the internal signal showing up in the cross-asset action through January and early February, but the trigger was exogenous (the COVID news flow) rather than purely positional. SKEW had been elevated through January, the small-cap index had been lagging since mid-November, and the regional bank index was already weak. The full correction unfolded in only six weeks, which is faster than the historical median, because the catalyst arrived sharper than the positioning had priced.

What the three episodes have in common is the joint pattern I described, which suggests that the joint signal I saw on Friday is consistent with a setup that historically precedes a meaningful equity drawdown by anywhere from four weeks to four months. The lead time depends on whether a catalyst arrives before the positioning resolves. Wednesday's NVDA print is the most plausible catalyst inside the next four weeks. If it does not trigger a regime change, the next plausible catalyst is the June FOMC meeting. If neither of those triggers the regime change, the third plausible catalyst is the July earnings season for the cover names. The historical analogs do not suggest that the distribution late-cycle setup resolves benignly. They suggest that the timing of the resolution depends on which catalyst arrives first.

The 1999 episode is sometimes brought up as an analog, and I want to be honest about why I am not using it as a close precedent. The dispersion in late 1999 was even more extreme than today, with the Nasdaq up +86 percent for the year while the equal-weight S&P 500 was up only +12 percent. But the 1999 episode resolved with a four-month melt-up before the actual peak in March 2000, and a melt-up scenario is one of the plausible bullish tail paths today. So while 1999 is a structural precedent for the mega-cap concentration mechanism, it is not a base case for the next four weeks. I am sizing my hedge to survive a melt-up before the eventual peak, which is part of why I chose August expirations and not June.

2. Why the Surface Did Not See It

The most surprising thing about Friday's session is not the magnitude of the internal moves. The most surprising thing is how little of it reached the headline index. SPY closed down 1.20 percent. SPX, the cash index, closed down approximately the same. Both still sit within 1.20 percent of the all-time high posted on Thursday May 14. The CBOE Volatility Index closed at 18.43, which would not have made headlines a year ago and barely registered in the financial press on Friday. CNN's Fear and Greed composite ended the day at 62.9, which the dashboard rounds to Greed. A reader who spent Friday morning at a soccer match and checked the close on their phone in the evening would have seen a normal pullback inside a strong rally, exactly what the headline writers wrote.

The reason the surface looks normal while the internals scream is a mechanical truth about how the modern S&P 500 is constructed. Eight stocks now account for a disproportionate share of the index by market capitalization, and seven of them outperformed the index by a wide margin over the last sixty trading days. When the marginal bid disappears from small caps and from cyclicals and from regional banks, the index can still print up if those eight names are bid. When the marginal bid disappears from those eight names too, the entire index breaks. The arithmetic is that simple. The arithmetic is also one-directional: when the eight names eventually break, there is no second layer of buyers to absorb the supply, because the second layer has been distributing for six months while the headlines tracked the cover.

The concentration math that nobody is doing

I want to walk you through the concentration arithmetic explicitly, because most coverage of mega-cap dispersion stops at the dispersion observation and never digs into the mechanical floor it creates and what it takes to break that floor. Let me lay it out in cold algebra.

The S&P 500 index is calculated by summing the market capitalization of each constituent and dividing by an index divisor. The contribution of any single name to a one-day index move is approximately that name's market cap weight times that name's one-day return. The top eight names by weight, as of Friday May 15, account for approximately 35 percent of the total index market cap, with Apple alone at roughly 7.2 percent, Microsoft at 6.8 percent, Nvidia at 6.5 percent, Amazon at 3.8 percent, Alphabet (combined classes) at 5.0 percent, Meta at 2.6 percent, Tesla at 1.7 percent, and Berkshire Hathaway at 1.9 percent. Numbers vary slightly day to day with market action. The remaining 492 names collectively account for the other 65 percent of the index.

The arithmetic of the cover mechanism works like this. If the top eight names rise by +1 percent as a group on a given day while the bottom 492 fall by an average of -0.5 percent, the index move is approximately (0.35 × +1.0%) + (0.65 × -0.5%) = +0.35% - 0.325% = +0.025%, which rounds to flat. The index would look as if nothing happened, even though 492 stocks declined meaningfully. This is the arithmetic by which a market can distribute under cover while the headline writers report stability.

The reason this matters today is that the dispersion has reached a level where a small reversal in the cover names produces an outsized headline move on the index. If on Wednesday the top eight names fall by -3 percent as a group while the bottom 492 fall by an additional -1.5 percent, the index moves by (0.35 × -3%) + (0.65 × -1.5%) = -1.05% - 0.975% = -2.025%, which is a meaningful headline decline that would force the financial press to acknowledge the regime shift. The asymmetry of the cover mechanism is that it works in both directions, and the same concentration that has held the index up for two months will produce a sharp index decline if the cover names crack together.

This is the reason NVDA on Wednesday matters more than NVDA on any normal earnings cycle. NVDA alone, at 6.5 percent of the index, can move the index by +0.4 to +0.5 percent on a +7 percent post-earnings gap, or by -0.4 to -0.5 percent on a -7 percent disappointment. But the second-order effect, in which a NVDA disappointment triggers selling pressure on the other seven cover names through factor-based unwinds, is potentially much larger. A 5 percent decline across the top eight names, triggered by a NVDA print that fails to lift the group, would produce a roughly 1.75 percent index decline before any breadth deterioration. Combine that with the existing internal weakness, and you get a 3 to 4 percent index decline in a single session, which would mark the largest single-day index decline since the tariff event of late March.

The Nifty Fifty parallel that holds

I said earlier that I do not lean on historical analogies, and I want to make an exception here for one specific case where the structural mechanics are tight enough to learn from. The Nifty Fifty episode of the early 1970s is the cleanest historical analog for the mega-cap concentration we see today, not because the names are similar but because the market structure created the same absorption mechanism we observe.

In 1972, a group of roughly fifty large-cap growth names (the Polaroids, the Xeroxes, the IBMs of their day) traded at average P/E ratios of 40 to 60 while the broader S&P 500 traded at 18. The dispersion was justified at the time by the same arguments used today: structural growth, sustainable competitive advantage, network effects in their respective verticals, durable cash flow that justified a permanent re-rating. The Nifty Fifty held up through the early stages of the 1973-1974 bear market, with the broader index already down by 15 to 20 percent before the Nifty Fifty group cracked. When they finally cracked, in the second half of 1973 and into 1974, the group fell by an average of 40 to 60 percent, with several names down more than 70 percent from their 1972 highs. Polaroid fell 90 percent. Xerox fell 70 percent. The names that delivered fundamentals in line with the bull thesis underperformed least; the names that delivered weak fundamentals underperformed most. But every one of them underperformed materially.

The structural parallel today is the AI capex cycle, which has produced a similar two-tier valuation pattern. The names that benefit most from AI capex (NVDA, AVGO, MU, ANET, ARM, SMCI, applicationally MSFT and GOOGL and META) trade at premium multiples that the market justifies on the basis of sustainable competitive advantage. The names that do not directly benefit have not seen the same multiple expansion. The dispersion is currently smaller in P/E terms than the Nifty Fifty era, but in absolute market-cap concentration terms it is similar or greater. The eight names at the top of the S&P 500 today account for more index weight than the entire Nifty Fifty did at the peak of their dominance.

The lesson from the Nifty Fifty is not that today's mega-caps will fall by 70 percent. The lesson is that concentration without breadth is a self-reinforcing setup that resolves with a sharp decline when the cover finally breaks, and that the decline is faster and deeper than the unwinding mechanics of equal-weight bear markets. The reason it is faster is that the index funds are the largest holders of the cover names, and the index funds sell mechanically on the way down as redemptions trigger them. The reason it is deeper is that there is no second layer of buyers to absorb the supply, because the second layer has been distributing for months and is mostly out by the time the cover cracks.

The FAANG 2018 analog

A second analog worth examining is the FAANG concentration of 2018, which culminated in the Q4 2018 drawdown that took the S&P 500 down 20 percent from its September peak. In the summer of 2018, the FAANG group accounted for roughly 15 percent of the index, which is less than half of today's mega-cap concentration. But the dispersion pattern in the months before the September peak was structurally similar. The small-cap index had been lagging since mid-August. The 10-year yield was rising from 2.85 percent to 3.25 percent. Regional banks were weakening. The defensives were not catching the rotation. SKEW was elevated.

The 2018 drawdown unfolded in three phases. Phase one was a gradual deterioration through October, with the S&P 500 down 8 percent by October 29. Phase two was a recovery rally through early November, with the S&P 500 back to within 3 percent of the September high by November 8. Phase three was a sharp decline from November 9 through December 24, with the S&P 500 down an additional 17 percent in seven weeks. The FAANG names underperformed during phase three by approximately 10 to 15 percentage points versus the index, exactly the asymmetric pattern that the Nifty Fifty episode predicts. The trade was to be defensive throughout phase one, to fade the phase two recovery, and to size up the bearish positioning into phase three.

What 2018 teaches us about today is that the most dangerous part of the late-cycle distribution is not the initial decline but the recovery rally that follows. The recovery rally re-engages the dip-buying retail bid, draws in the systematic flows, and creates the conditions for a deeper second leg lower. The dip-buyers who win the next twelve months are not the dip-buyers who buy the first 5 percent. They are the dip-buyers who recognize that the first 5 percent is the bait, and wait for the failed recovery before deploying capital. I am writing this paragraph in part as a discipline check for myself, because the temptation to redeploy after a clean 5 percent decline is real, and the historical record suggests it is almost always a mistake to act on that temptation before the regime fully resolves.
