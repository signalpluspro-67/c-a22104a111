The Jobs Report the Bulls Didn't Want
Good News, Bad Friday. The Calm Finally Broke.
172,000 Jobs, One Ugly Friday, and a Yen at 160

Hello my friends,

Last week I told you the top had been postponed. I wrote that the loud, fast catalysts I had been watching, the war and the double top, had resolved the wrong way, and that what was left on the bearish side was the patient stuff: concentration, complacency, and a yen sitting one candle from its line. I also told you the surface was calm while the tail was quietly being bid, and that the gap between those two things was the part of the tape keeping my hand near the exit.

This Friday the gap closed. The May jobs report came in at 172,000 against expectations of barely 85,000, more than double, with the prior two months revised up by another 93,000 on top of that. A hot labor print is good news for the economy and bad news for a market that had spent nine straight weeks pricing rate cuts. Yields ripped, the ten-year pushed back above 4.5%, and the odds of a Fed hike this year jumped from a quarter to better than half on the betting markets. The index that printed fresh records on Thursday gave back 2.6% on Friday. The Nasdaq fell 4.2%, its worst day in over a year, and roughly a trillion dollars came off the semiconductor and AI complex in a single session. The nine-week winning streak is over.

So here is the summary up front. The patient bear case I described last week did not stay patient. It found its catalyst, and the catalyst was the one I had not been leading with: not a war, not a chart pattern, but the simplest macro fact there is, which is that the labor market is too strong for the rate cuts this market was leaning on. Every tension I catalogued for you last week fired in the same afternoon. But notice the shape of the selling, because it matters more than the headline, and it is the reason my hand is steady and not slamming the exit.

📊 Market Health

Here is the most important thing I can tell you about Friday, and it is the thing the index number hides. The S&P fell 2.6% and the Nasdaq fell 4.2%, but the median stock barely moved. Across my universe the typical fourteen-day RSI finished the week around 52, almost exactly where it sat a week ago, and well over half of stocks held above their fifty-day line. A 2.6% down day that leaves the average company intact is not a broad liquidation. It is a surgical one.

The selling was surgical. It hit the crowded mega-cap and AI trade, not the average stock.

What came off was the froth, not the foundation. The share of my universe reading overbought, the stretched, extended, everybody-already-owns-it names, dropped by roughly half on the week, from about 10% down toward 5%. Read those two facts together and you get the story of Friday. The selling concentrated in the exact mega-cap and semiconductor names that had carried the entire advance, while the breadth of the average company held. For two months I have been warning you that this rally was dangerously narrow, that a handful of names were doing all the work. This week the market started letting the air out of precisely those names, and the rest of the field did not follow them down. That is concentration beginning to unwind, and as ugly as a 4% Nasdaq day feels, an unwind that spares the broad market is healthier than it looks.

The honest caveat: one Friday is not a trend. The froth coming off the top is constructive only if the field keeps holding. If next week the selling broadens and the median stock starts rolling over with the leaders, then this was the first crack and not a healthy rotation. I am watching the breadth, not the index, for that answer.

📉 Volatility

This is where the week showed its teeth. The VIX closed at 21.5, up from 15.3 the Friday before, a 40% jump and the highest reading in two months. After three straight weeks of compression that I described to you as deepening calm, the calm did not fade gently. It snapped.

The fear that sat quietly in the tail for weeks hit the surface in a single session.

Look at the near-term gauge, because it tells you this was an event and not a drift. The nine-day VIX went from under 13 on Thursday to nearly 24 on Friday, almost doubling in a session. That is the signature of a market repricing risk around a specific catalyst in real time, not a slow accumulation of worry. The vol-of-vol, VVIX, jumped from the mid-80s to 102, which means traders were not just buying protection, they were paying up for protection on their protection. And my complacency gauge, the VIX3M/VIX ratio that I told you last week was grinding toward the 1.30 ceiling where tops form, did not just stop climbing. It collapsed, from 1.22 to almost exactly 1.00. The term structure flattened to the edge of backwardation, which is what fear looks like in the plumbing, when near-dated insurance suddenly costs as much as the far-dated kind.

And remember the one number that did not fit the calm last week, the SKEW index sitting up near 144 while everything else dozed. I told you someone was quietly paying up for tail protection while spot vol melted to a three-week low. This week the spot caught up to the tail. The crash insurance that looked expensive and lonely a week ago looks prescient now. The paranoid bid hiding in the SKEW was early, and early is just right with a lag.

🔍 Pairs Alignment

QQQ versus equal-weight. The concentration read I have hammered for weeks finally moved in the right direction, and it took a selloff to do it. The Nasdaq-100 fell while the equal-weight S&P actually held green on the week. For the first time in two months the gap between the cap-weighted engine and the average stock narrowed instead of widening, because the leaders gave back more than the field. That is the mechanism of a concentration unwind, and it is exactly the rebalancing I have been telling you this market needed.

QQQ versus IWM. Small caps held up better than the Nasdaq this week, down only fractionally while tech took the brunt. The most rate-sensitive corner of the market did not lead the selloff, which is mildly surprising on a day when the entire story was higher yields. It tells me the selling was about positioning in the crowded trade more than a clean rate repricing across the board. If this were a pure rate shock, the small caps would have broken first. They did not.

Stocks versus bonds. This is the one that confirms my read, and it is the one I want you to sit with. In a normal risk-off day, Treasuries catch a bid, yields fall, and bonds cushion the equity drop. That is not what happened. Bonds fell with stocks. The long-Treasury proxy dropped on the week, yields rose, the dollar firmed back above 100, and gold lost 3%. Stocks down, bonds down, dollar up, gold down, all at once. That is not the signature of a growth scare, where money floods into Treasuries for safety. It is the signature of a positioning unwind, of leverage coming out of the system together. For two months I told you the bond market was refusing to confirm the rally and that I would bet on the bond market being right. This week the bond market got its catalyst and the stock market blinked.

Out of rate-sensitive growth, into defensives and value. The textbook response to a hot jobs print.

Connect it to the news, because they are the same event. A hot jobs report pushes the rate path higher for longer. Higher-for-longer is poison for anything priced on discounted future cash flows, which is precisely the mega-cap technology and AI complex that led this whole advance. And higher-for-longer is a tailwind for banks, who earn the spread, and a non-event for healthcare and energy, which sell things people buy regardless of the funds rate. So money did the logical thing. It left the names with the most rate sensitivity and the most stretched valuations, and it walked into the names with real earnings and no duration risk. Health care in particular has quietly become a leadership group, and the managed-care names inside it have gone from the most hated corner of the market a year ago to one of its strongest. Hold that thought, because it is where my trade this week lives.

The Discretionary-versus-Staples read confirms the turn. Last week I told you Discretionary was edging ahead of Staples, the fingerprint of an offensive consumer. This week it reversed. Discretionary underperformed and Staples held flat, the consumer leaning back to defense. A small reading, in the right direction for the week's story.

💱 FX

And now the chart I have called the single most important in the world for weeks, the one I keep going to bed thinking about. USDJPY closed Friday at 160.2. It broke 160.

I need you to feel the weight of that, because I have written about this line in every letter for over a month. One hundred sixty is the level where Tokyo has historically turned from talking to acting. Japan spent more than 11 trillion yen, over seventy billion dollars, defending the currency between late April and late May, its first intervention since 2024. The yen has now erased every bit of that defense and pushed back through the line anyway. The Finance Minister repeated this week that the authorities stand ready to act. The market pushed it through 160 regardless.

The mechanism has not changed. You borrow yen at near-zero, convert to dollars, and buy higher-yielding US assets, increasingly the same mega-cap tech where the momentum and the liquidity live. As long as the yen drifts weak, the trade prints. My working thesis remains that this cheap-yen funding and the equity concentration in the sector table are not two stories but one trade wearing two faces. And here is why this Friday was more dangerous than a normal down day. The jobs report that pushed US yields higher also widened the rate gap between America and Japan, which pushes the yen weaker, which pushes USDJPY further through the level that invites intervention. The same catalyst that hit equities also wound the FX spring tighter.

The asymmetry is the whole game now. Above 160, every day that passes without intervention is a day the carry trade grows bolder and the concentration we already have gets one more push. But the higher it climbs, the louder the case for Tokyo to act, and a sudden intervention or a BoJ move is exactly the trigger that forces leveraged carry positions to unwind. Unwinding means selling the US assets they bought, which means selling the very mega-cap tech that just had its worst day in a year. August of 2024 was the rehearsal: a modest BoJ hike produced one of the most violent moves in the yen and one of the sharpest equity air pockets in years. We are not there. Last week the line was one candle away. This week we are standing on it.

🧠 My Take

The weight between my two stories shifted hard this week, and for once it shifted toward the side I have been leaning the whole time. I am not taking a victory lap, because the discipline that let me see this coming is the same discipline that tells me not to overplay a single Friday.

The cautious story, which is now the louder one. The labor market is too strong for the rate cuts this market was built on, and that is not a headline that resolves in a day. Yields are up, hike odds are above half, the dollar is firm, and the most expensive, most crowded part of the market just took its first real hit. The complacency gauge collapsed, near-term vol doubled, and the yen broke the line I have watched for a month. That is a coherent, mechanical, this-actually-matters bear case, and unlike last week's it is no longer slow and patient. It has a catalyst with a date on it: the next inflation print, the next jobs report, the next BoJ meeting.

The story I still have to respect. The selling was surgical, not broad. The field held. Bonds did not signal recession, they signaled a rate repricing, which is a different and more survivable thing. And an economy that just produced 172,000 jobs is, by definition, not falling apart. This could be a healthy flush, the market wringing speculative excess out of the mega-cap leadership while the broad economy keeps humming. If the field keeps holding and the dip-buyers show up in the unglamorous, profitable parts of the market, this resolves as a rotation and not a top.

So here is where I sit, and it is almost the mirror image of last week. Last week the data leaned constructive and pulled me, reluctantly, off my caution. This week the data leaned cautious and confirmed the discipline that kept me in cash. I still hold the heaviest cash position of my year, and this week I am glad of it. I am not selling into the hole, because the field is holding and panic-selling a 2.6% down day is how you turn a flush into a mistake. But I am also not chasing whatever bounce Monday brings. What I am doing is exactly what I said I would: putting cash to work selectively, in the parts of the market that just proved they do not need the Fed to cut, one careful position at a time. The shopping list this week lives in the green columns of that sector table.

Cautious (~55%). The jobs report is the first of a series. The next inflation or labor print keeps rates higher, the yen forces Tokyo's hand, and the carry unwind turns this surgical selloff into a broad one. VIX3M/VIX stays pinned near 1.00 or inverts, the field finally rolls over to join the leaders, and the flush becomes a correction. This is the path where the single point of failure I have flagged all month finally gives way.

Constructive (~45%). Friday was a positioning flush, not a regime change. The hot labor market reassures more than it frightens, the broad economy keeps the field bid, and money completes its rotation out of overpriced tech into reasonably-priced everything else. The market broadens into a healthier, less concentrated advance. The top names needed to come in. They came in. The market is better for it.

🔥 Trade of the Week: CI (Cigna)

I will say it before the trade, the same as always: my cash position is the heaviest of my year and I am not abandoning that discipline for one name. But the entire letter this week points in one direction, toward the rate-insensitive, real-earnings parts of the market that just outperformed while the crowded trade broke. And the cleanest expression of that idea is sitting in the leading sector, hiding in plain sight as the one name in the group that has not run.

My screens this week were full of managed-care momentum. Humana and UnitedHealth both flagged across multiple signals. But both are at fresh fifty-two-week highs, and Humana's fourteen-day RSI is at 74, stretched and extended exactly like the mega-cap names I am refusing to chase at the top. Buying the leader of a hot group at its high, in the week the market started punishing stretched names, is the wrong lesson to take from this tape. So I am doing what the letter tells me to do. I am buying the laggard, not the leader.

Cigna sits about 14% below its fifty-two-week high while its peers print new ones. Its RSI is a neutral 51, it is consolidating right on top of its fifty-day and two-hundred-day lines, which have converged into a tight support shelf near 280, and it has spent the last month basing in the high 260s to high 280s rather than going parabolic. It is the rare thing I want in this tape: a defensive, rate-insensitive business with real earnings, in the sector money is rotating into, that has not already made its move.

The thesis sits in three layers.

First, the business. Cigna is a managed-care and pharmacy-benefits giant with real, growing earnings. It beat in April, $7.79 against $7.60, and guides to better than $30 of earnings this year, which puts the stock under ten times earnings. This is not a multiple-on-a-dream that needs cheap money to justify itself. It is a cash machine that gets cheaper, not more expensive, when rates rise, because nobody is discounting its earnings out to 2040. In the week the market repriced the cost of money, that is exactly the kind of balance sheet I want to own.

Second, the setup. The fifty and two-hundred-day lines have converged near 280, which gives the stop a clean and obvious place to live. The base in the high 260s has held on multiple tests over the past month, the most recent low around 268. RSI at 51 is neutral and turning, not stretched, so I am not chasing strength, I am buying a coiled spring in a leading group. And the next earnings report is not until July 30, well outside my swing window, so there is no binary event to detonate the setup. This is consolidation with a turn underneath it, not a falling knife.

Third, the asymmetry. Entry in the mid-280s, a stop just under 268 where the base and both moving averages give way together, which risks about 6%. The first target is the recent shelf near 300, and the real prize is the round trip toward the prior high near 334, with the average analyst target sitting around 345. That is better than a two-to-one payoff, and the downside is defined by a support structure that has already been tested rather than by hope. If the rotation into defensive healthcare has legs, the laggard with the most room to run goes first. If the whole tape wobbles on the yen, a cheap, rate-insensitive cash machine is exactly where I want my risk parked.

The honest risk: Cigna lags its peers for a reason, and the reason has a name, the perennial regulatory overhang on pharmacy-benefit managers. Barclays cut it to neutral two weeks ago on exactly that concern. If Washington reopens the PBM file, this is a name that can stay cheap longer than the chart suggests. I size it as a position, not a bet.

One housekeeping note on last week's idea. CELH, the Celsius bottom I flagged at 33 with a base at 27 to 28, fell back this week and now sits near 29, right on the floor I identified. The base has not broken, but the trade has not worked either. I am treating 27 as the line. It holds, the thesis stays intact and patient. It breaks, I am out. I tell you when they work and when they do not.

🧘 The Philosopher's Corner

Last week, in this same corner, I wrote about holding a view loosely enough to let the evidence move it. That week the data leaned constructive and I let it pull me off my caution. This week the data leaned the other way and confirmed it. The temptation, after a week like this, is to feel vindicated, and vindication is its own kind of trap, because a man who feels proven right stops looking.

Seneca had a line for it. He wrote that we suffer more in imagination than in reality. The market spent two months suffering my imagined tops that never came, and then the real one, when it finally arrived, looked nothing like the picture I had drawn. It was not a war and it was not a chart pattern. It was a good jobs number. The lesson is not that I was right to be cautious. The lesson is that the thing which finally moves the tape is almost never the thing you spent your energy fearing. So I will hold this week's bearish lean the same way I held last week's bullish one. Firmly enough to act on, loosely enough to drop the moment the data turns again. The truth never harmed anyone. Persisting in error does.
