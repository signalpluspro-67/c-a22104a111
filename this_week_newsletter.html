So this week is a progress report and a warning in the same breath. The internals confirmed the read. The calendar handed me a reason to stay careful. Here is what the market told me, and why I am still holding cash into a central bank meeting.

📊 Market Health

This is the section that settles last week's call, so start here. The index went nowhere and the market underneath it got healthier. That combination is the whole story.

Start with the headline. SPY closed the week at 738.37, up about 0.1% from the prior Friday. Flat. A week of noise that netted to nothing at the index level. But the index is a weighted average run by a handful of giants, and a flat print at the top can hide almost anything underneath. This week it hid strength.

Look at participation, the number I keep coming back to. The share of stocks trading above their 50-day moving average climbed from 53.1% at the start of the week to 58.7% by Friday. The 50-day line is the standard intermediate-trend filter, and when more stocks push back above it the broad uptrend is getting stronger. The index stalled and the participation underneath it grew. That is the opposite of how a top behaves.

Then the retest, which is where the week earned its keep. On Wednesday, June 10, SPY dipped to about 725 intraday, the low of the week, a clean gut-check of the prior lows. Then it bought it back. Over the next two sessions it climbed straight off that low to close green for the week. A real top does not repurchase its own retest in forty-eight hours. Friday's tape backed it up, with advancing stocks beating decliners better than two to one and new highs outnumbering new lows.

Here is why this matters so much for the call I made. A real top is a slow process. It builds over weeks of quiet internal decay, the breadth falling away from the price long before the index finally gives in. What we got was the inverse, and now twice running. The price took the hit on June 5 and the breadth refused to follow. Then the price went flat and the breadth improved anyway. Last week's flush read now has a full week of confirming internals stacked behind it. The market voted, and it voted my way.

One honest caveat, the same one I always give you. Breadth is a snapshot. It does not promise next week. A widening this week does not guarantee a widening next week, and next week carries an event I will get to. But the job this weekend was to check whether the breadth held the line I drew, and it did, with room to spare.

📉 Volatility

If Market Health tells you what the market did, volatility tells you how it felt while doing it. This week every gauge says the same thing. The scare got absorbed.

The VIX, the market's 30-day expected-volatility gauge, peaked at 22.2 on Wednesday's dip and settled back to 17.68 by Friday. The panic spiked into the retest and then drained out of the tape as the market bought the low. That is a fear gauge doing exactly what it does in a flush, jumping on the gut-check and falling as the gut-check holds.

The front end tells it cleaner. The VIX9D, which measures expected volatility over just the next nine days, had nearly doubled into the June 5 flush. This week it collapsed from 23.9 to 17.3. The acute, right-now fear fully drained, and the 9-day reading now sits a touch below the 30-day VIX. That is the calm-market configuration, the short end quieter than the medium end, the shape you see after a shock has passed rather than while one is building.

The complacency ratio confirms it. I track VIX3M divided by VIX, three-month expected vol over one-month. At the June 5 panic it had collapsed to 1.01, right at the edge of backwardation, near-term fear caught up to medium-term fear. This week it re-steepened back to 1.16. The market rebuilt its layer of calm instead of spiraling lower. That re-steepening was the base case I laid out last week, and it played out on schedule.

One gauge keeps me honest, and it is the same one that has kept me honest for weeks. SKEW, the price of crash insurance, eased from 152 to 142.6. Lower, and still elevated. The professional bid for downside protection cooled as the market recovered, and it never went away. That standing bid is the market quietly pricing a tail I am pricing too, and this week that tail has a name and a date. The yen, and the Bank of Japan.

🔍 Pairs Alignment

The cleanest way to see whether a move is broad or narrow is to put assets side by side and watch which one led. Last week the pairs told a story of a rates shock hitting the longest-duration assets. This week they tell the recovery half of the same story, and every pair that broke against me mended.

The flushed growth bounced hardest. The Nasdaq rose 1.24% on the week against the Dow's 0.52%. The exact complex that took the June 5 hit, the crowded mega-cap and AI names, led the rebound. That is how a positioning flush reverses. The trade that got unwound on the way down is the trade that snaps back when the selling exhausts, because the selling was forced rather than fundamental.

Breadth beat the giants again. The equal-weight S&P rose 1.36% while the cap-weighted index added 0.1%. For a second straight week the average stock outran the handful of giants at the top. The concentration that has worried me all year kept unwinding in the healthy direction, the leaders coming back to the pack rather than the pack falling to the leaders.

Small caps led everything. IWM, the Russell 2000, rose 3.64%, the best of the board. The most rate-sensitive, highest-beta corner of the market, the one hit hardest in the flush, rebounded the most as the pressure that caused the damage eased. The 10-year yield slipped from 4.54% to 4.49%, a small move that took the boot off the throat of the rate-sensitive trades, and they ran.

Stocks and bonds stopped falling together. Last week the tell was bonds selling off alongside stocks, the signature of a rates shock. This week TLT, the long-dated Treasury proxy, firmed about 0.6% as yields slipped. The rates shock is cooling rather than compounding. When the thing that broke the tape starts to mend, the tape mends with it.

🚨 Sector Rotation

To know whether money was fleeing or shopping, you watch where it went. This week it went back into exactly what got flushed.

Eleven of twelve sectors closed green. Lead the list with the names that took the worst of the flush. Biotech, the XBI, rose 3.7% on the week after falling nearly 6% in the flush week. The corner that got hit hardest came back the hardest. Materials rose 2.7%. Staples rose 2.7%. A market putting money into beaten-down speculative biotech and steady defensive staples in the same week is a market shopping both ends of the aisle, covering its bases, rotating rather than fleeing.

The one red sector is the tell that confirms it. Energy fell 0.8%, the only loser on the board, and Energy was the leader the week before. The money did not leave the market. It rotated out of last week's winner and into last week's losers. That is the footprint of a healthy rotation inside a market that is staying put, the opposite of a bear market, which sells everything at once and paints the whole board red.

💱 FX

Now the part where I stop building the bull case and tell you, without hedging, where this can still go wrong. Everything above is constructive. One thing is not, and it sits on a single currency pair. USDJPY closed the week at 160.2, exactly where it sat all week, glued to the line.

The level 160 is not arbitrary. It is the line where the Bank of Japan and the Ministry of Finance have stepped in to defend the yen, because a yen that falls too far too fast destabilizes Japan and ripples out through global funding markets. They defended it. Between late April and late May, Japan spent over ¥11.7 trillion, more than $73 billion, its first intervention since 2024. The market erased all of it and pinned the yen on the broken line anyway. That is a market testing a central bank, and the central bank meets next week.

Here is why one currency pair can override a whole tape of healthy internals. The yen carry trade is one of the largest funding engines in global markets. For years money has borrowed in yen at near-zero cost and bought higher-yielding assets all over the world, including US tech. A weak, stable yen keeps that trade printing and keeps the borrowed money flowing into risk. The danger is a violent snap-back the other way. If the Bank of Japan hikes next week, or signals it is done waiting, and the yen lurches back toward 155, the carry unwind turns live and the borrowed positioning that funded years of risk-taking gets pulled at once. We saw the rehearsal in August 2024, when a single modest BoJ hike took down equities worldwide in days. The spark next week could be larger.

🧠 My Take

Last week I split it 45% constructive and 55% cautious, and I told you the only reason the cautious case got the larger share was the yen sitting on a broken line while the breadth was still unproven. This week the breadth proved itself. Three of the four things I told you to watch broke my way. So I move my odds toward the constructive side, while keeping real weight on the risk that has not resolved.

Scenario one, constructive, about 55%. The flush is confirmed and the rotation keeps broadening. The Bank of Japan holds, or hikes gently and signals patience, and the yen stays orderly through the meeting. The rates shock that started all of this keeps cooling, small caps and the beaten-down growth keep mending, and the index grinds back toward its highs on better internals than it had at the top. This is the case the internal evidence supports most directly, and it is now my base case. The only thing keeping it from a bigger number is next week's meeting.

Scenario two, cautious, about 45%. The Bank of Japan surprises hawkish, or the market reads the meeting as the start of faster tightening, and the yen snaps back toward 155. The carry unwind goes live, the borrowed money that funded global risk gets pulled at once, and the healthy breadth I am leaning on breaks fast as a forced deleveraging spreads from one corner into all of them. I hold this as a real probability for one reason, the BoJ meeting, not because the tape this week gave me anything to fear. The tape this week was clean. The calendar is the risk.

What that means in practice. Constructive at 55% and cautious at 45% is a green light to participate, with one hand on the exit. I added to the rotation this week on the breadth confirmation. I am holding cash through the BoJ meeting. And I am ready to cut fast if the yen forces the issue. Lean in on the confirmation, stay light into the event.

🔥 Trade of the Week: CI (Cigna)

I am keeping last week's pick, because it did exactly what I asked it to do. Cigna, the managed-care and pharmacy-benefit giant, was my contrarian buy last week, the quality laggard left behind while its whole peer group ran to fresh highs. This week the sector bid found it. CI rose 2.7% to close at 297.29, walking straight up toward my first target of 300 from the entry near 286, and it did it the disciplined way.

Look at the difference between CI and the names everyone was chasing. HUM ran another 6.2% this week to a fresh 52-week high with an RSI of 82.7, deep into overbought. That is the stretched leader I told you to leave alone, the one with the most room to fall and the least room to run. CI is the opposite. Its RSI sits at 60.3, woken up off neutral and nowhere near overbought, with the 50-day at 282.68 and the 200-day at 281.66 converged right underneath it as support. The quiet laggard caught the same sector bid as the overbought leader, with a fraction of the downside risk.

The plan is unchanged and it is working. Entry near 286, stop just under 268, below the recent base low of 270.43, about 6% of risk. First target 300, nearly tagged at Friday's 297.29. The real prize is the move back toward the prior high near 339, with the average analyst target above that. CI is 12% below its 52-week high while its peers print new ones, which is the whole reason the reward against the risk is worth taking. Next earnings are July 30, outside the swing window, so there is no binary print to hold through.

The honest risk has not changed either. The pharmacy-benefit business is a recurring political target, and a single headline about reform or pricing can hit the whole group regardless of fundamentals. That overhang is part of why the stock is cheap and part of why it lags. The stop just under 268 is my answer. If the base breaks, I am out around a 6% loss and I move on. It is working so far. The line tells me when it stops working.

🧘 The Philosopher's Corner

Marcus Aurelius kept a rule for moments like the one I sat in last weekend. When something frightening came at him, he made himself wait and look at the thing plainly, separate from the story his mind wanted to wrap around it. Last week the feed handed me a story, the worst Nasdaq day in a year, the top is in, sell everything. The discipline was to set the story down and wait for the one thing that would actually decide it. Not the headline. The breadth. So I waited, and this week the breadth gave its verdict, and the verdict was flush.

Seneca wrote that we suffer more often in imagination than in reality, and the imagination is always louder at the turn. The crowd traded its fear last Friday and most of that fear turned out to be imaginary. The retest got bought in two days. Eleven of twelve sectors closed green. The average stock climbed back above its trend line. The reality was quieter and kinder than the story, the way it usually is when the story is that unanimous.

Here is the part I have to hold onto, because patience is not a victory lap. Being right last week buys me nothing next week. The same discipline that told me to wait for the breadth now tells me to wait for the Bank of Japan. I do not get to take the win and stop watching. The job is the same on a green week as on a red one. Set the story down, find the one thing that actually decides it, and wait for the thing to speak. The breadth spoke. Now the yen gets its turn. I will be sitting here, cash in reserve, listening for it.
