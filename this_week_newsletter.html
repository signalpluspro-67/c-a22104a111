Everyone Saw a Top. I Saw a Flush.

The index fell hard and the average stock barely moved. That is not how tops begin.
Market Recap, June 6th 2026

Hello my friends. By the time you read this, your feed is already a wall of the same word, and that word is "top." Friday gave everyone the headline they have been waiting all year to write. The Nasdaq fell 4.2%, its worst single day in over a year, and roughly $1 trillion evaporated off the semiconductor and AI complex in a few hours. The S&P 500 dropped 2.6% and snapped a nine-week winning streak that had carried it to fresh records on Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday. So the story writes itself. The melt-up cracked, the leaders broke, the bell rang at the top, and the smart move is to sell everything and hide. Half the Substacks you follow published some version of that thesis before the closing bell had even finished printing.

I read it differently, and I want to walk you through exactly why, because the gap between what the index did and what the market did this week is the widest I have seen in months. The index is one number. It is a cap-weighted average dominated by a handful of enormous names, and when those specific names get hit, the index screams even if nothing else moves. That is precisely what happened Friday. The damage was not spread across the tape. It was concentrated, almost surgically, in the exact crowded mega-cap and AI names that every fast-money desk and every retail account already owned to the gills. Meanwhile, underneath that screaming headline number, the median stock barely flinched. More than half the market closed the week above its 50-day line. The advance-decline picture stayed intact. The froth came off the top of the glass and the beer underneath is still there.

So here is the read I am putting my name on this weekend, against the crowd. This was a flush, not a top. It was a positioning unwind in the most over-owned corner of the market, and the cross-asset tape confirms it down to the last detail. When a real top forms, the rot spreads. The average stock rolls over weeks before the index does, breadth deteriorates quietly while the headline holds up, and the bond market bids hard as growth fears take over. None of that happened. The breadth held, the rot did not spread, and bonds fell right alongside stocks instead of catching a safety bid. That last point is the tell, and we will get to it. The crowd is reading the index and calling it the market. I am reading the market, and the market is telling me to go shopping in the parts of it that nobody got hurt in.

The contrarian case in two sentences, before we open the hood. The selling was surgical, it hit the crowded AI and mega-cap names while the broad field of stocks held above its 50-day line, and a concentration unwind of that kind is a healthy rebalancing rather than the start of a collapse. I am using this dip to add selectively in the rate-insensitive, real-earnings parts of the market that got cheaper for no reason of their own, while keeping one clear, honest eye on the single risk that could prove me wrong, which is the yen breaking 160.

📊 Market Health

This section is the whole case, so I am going to spend real time here. If you take nothing else from this letter, take what is in this part, because the entire bull-versus-bear argument for this weekend lives in the difference between two numbers that almost nobody is putting side by side: the index return and the median stock's return.

Start with the index. The S&P 500 fell 2.6% on Friday and the Nasdaq fell 4.2%. Those are big, scary, attention-grabbing prints, and they are real. But an index is a weighted average, and the S&P in 2026 is more top-heavy than it has been in modern history. A handful of trillion-dollar names carry an outsized share of the move. When those specific stocks get sold, the index drops hard regardless of what the other 480-odd companies are doing. So the index return on a day like Friday tells you what happened to the giants. It does not tell you what happened to the market. For that you have to look underneath, and underneath is where the story flips.

Look at the median 14-day RSI. It sat around 52 going into the week and it came out around 52. It barely moved. Think about what that means for a second. RSI is a momentum gauge that runs from 0 to 100, where 50 is dead neutral, readings above 70 are stretched and overbought, and readings below 30 are washed out and oversold. A median RSI parked at 52 across the whole universe is the definition of a market in balance. If Friday had been the start of a genuine broad collapse, the median RSI would have cratered. Hundreds of stocks would have rolled over together, the breadth would have caved, and that median number would be sitting in the low 40s or worse by Friday's close. It did not budge. The typical stock in the market is exactly where it was a week ago. That is not what the inside of a top looks like.

Now the participation number, which is the one I keep coming back to. Over half of all stocks, about 58%, closed the week above their 50-day moving average. The 50-day line is the standard intermediate-trend filter. When a stock is above it, the trend of the last couple of months is constructive. When the majority of the market is above it, the broad uptrend is healthy regardless of what any single index print says on any single day. Friday took the Nasdaq apart, and yet 58% of stocks held their intermediate trend line. Sit with that contradiction, because it is the engine of my whole thesis. The market that the headline describes, a market in collapse, and the market that the breadth describes, a market holding its trend, cannot both be true. One of them is an illusion. I am telling you the headline is the illusion.

Then there is the froth gauge, and this is where the word "flush" earns its keep. Going into the week, the share of stocks in overbought territory was about 10%. By Friday's close it had fallen to about 5%. So the overbought share got cut in half. Read that carefully, because it is the cleanest evidence that what happened was a release of pressure, not the onset of damage. The selling removed the stretched, extended, over-loved names from their overbought perch and dropped them back toward neutral. It did not push the broad market into oversold capitulation. It vented the froth off the top and left the body of the market intact. A correction that takes the overbought count from 10% to 5% while leaving the median RSI at 52 and 58% of stocks above their 50-day is the very picture of a healthy internal reset. It is the market exhaling.

Put those four readings together and you get a single, coherent diagnosis that runs directly against the consensus. Median RSI at 52, unchanged. Participation at 58% above the 50-day, intact. Overbought share cut from 10% to 5%, froth vented. And an index down 2.6% to 4.2% that, by every internal measure, did not drag the field down with it. The selling was surgical. It was a scalpel, not a sledgehammer, and it cut out exactly the tissue that was most inflamed: the crowded mega-cap AI complex that everyone piled into, the names sitting at the highest overbought readings, the trades that had the most hot money leaning on them. Those got cut. The rest of the market sat there and watched.

Here is why that distinction is everything. Tops are processes, not events. A real top is built over weeks of quiet internal decay. The leaders keep making new highs in the index while, underneath, fewer and fewer stocks participate. The average stock peaks and starts rolling over while the headline holds up on the backs of three or four giants. Breadth diverges from price for a month or two, and only then does the index finally give way, by which point the damage under the surface is already done. That is the anatomy of 2000, of 2007, of every meaningful top. The breadth leads the price down. What we got Friday is the exact inverse. The price led and the breadth refused to follow. The index cracked in a single session and the field of stocks stood its ground. That is not a top forming. That is a crowded trade unwinding while the market underneath stays healthy.

📉 Volatility

If Market Health tells you what the market did, volatility tells you how the market felt while it did it, and Friday's volatility move fits the flush read like a glove. Fear spiked, hard and fast, in exactly the pattern of a positioning shock that burns hot and then fades, rather than the slow, grinding fear that compounds into something worse.

The VIX, the market's 30-day expected-volatility gauge, ran from 15.3 the prior Friday to 21.5 by this Friday's close. That is a 40% jump and a two-month high. On its own it sounds alarming, and the crowd is reading it as confirmation of the apocalypse. But look at the front end, because the front end is where the panic actually showed up. The VIX9D, which measures expected volatility over just the next nine days, went from 12.6 to 23.9. It nearly doubled in a single session. When the 9-day reading rockets past the 30-day reading like that, the term structure is screaming acute, immediate, right-now fear. That is the fingerprint of a shock, a sudden scramble for short-dated protection in response to one event, in this case one jobs report. It is not the fingerprint of a market settling into a sustained downtrend, which would show up as a steady grind higher across the whole curve rather than a violent spike at the very front.

The complacency ratio tells the same story. I track VIX3M divided by VIX, the ratio of three-month expected vol to one-month. Going into the week it sat at 1.22, a steep contango that signals a calm, complacent market pricing the near term as quieter than the medium term. By Friday it had flattened to about 1.01, right at the edge of backwardation. When that ratio collapses toward and below 1, near-term fear has caught up to and nearly overtaken medium-term fear. That is a classic shock signature, and it is also, historically, the kind of reading that tends to mean-revert. Spikes in near-term vol relative to the longer end usually fade as the immediate scare passes, the term structure re-steepens, and the complacency ratio climbs back toward contango. That fade is the trade, and it only fails if the field breaks, which, per the last section, it has not.

VVIX, the volatility of volatility, went from the mid-80s to about 102. This one matters and I want to be straight about it, because it cuts slightly against my own read and I am not going to hide that. VVIX measures the price of convexity, how much the market is paying for options on volatility itself. A jump into the low 100s says the move was not entirely dismissed, that some real money reached for tail protection and the demand for convexity picked up. I would have preferred to see VVIX stay floored, because a floored vol-of-vol would say the shock was being shrugged off entirely. It did not stay floored. So I am giving that its due weight: the spike was respected, not laughed off. That said, low 100s on VVIX is elevated, not extreme. It is the reading of a market that got a scare and bought some insurance, not the reading of a market in the grip of a convexity panic where everyone is scrambling for tails at any price.

Then there is SKEW, and SKEW is the piece that ties this volatility section back to everything I told you last week. SKEW measures the price of out-of-the-money put protection relative to at-the-money, essentially how much the market is paying up for crash insurance. It sat around 144 going into the week and it stayed elevated right through Friday. Here is the thing about that. SKEW has been bid for weeks. It was high before Friday, while the surface was dead calm and the VIX was sleeping at 15.3. Last week, in "The Top Got Postponed," I told you exactly this, that the surface was calm while the tail was quietly bid, that someone was paying for crash insurance even as the index made new highs day after day. That elevated SKEW was the market whispering that a pocket of fragility existed under the calm. Friday, that fragility got expressed, in the one crowded corner where it was concentrated. The tail that was bid for weeks paid off in the AI complex, and now the question is whether the rest of the surface follows it down or whether the shock stays contained. The breadth says contained.

🔍 Pairs Alignment

The cleanest way to see whether the damage was concentrated or broad is to put pairs of indices and assets side by side and watch which one cracked and which one held. Three pairs tell the whole story this week, and all three confirm the flush read rather than the top read.

QQQ versus equal-weight (RSP): the concentration gap finally narrowed, and that is the rebalancing you wanted. This is the one I care about most. QQQ, the Nasdaq-100, fell 1.1% on the week. RSP, the S&P 500 equal-weight index where the smallest company counts the same as the largest, actually rose 0.6% on the week. Read that again, because it is remarkable. In the same week that the headlines screamed collapse, the equal-weight version of the market closed green. The cap-weighted, mega-cap-dominated, AI-heavy QQQ took the hit while the democratic, every-stock-counts-the-same RSP gained ground. For months I have been writing about the extreme concentration in this market, QQQ stretched far out ahead of equal-weight, the whole index leaning on a handful of giants, a top-heavy structure that needed to rebalance. This week it started to rebalance. The money came out of the crowded few and the broad many held and even gained. That is not the market breaking. That is the market healing the exact imbalance I have been worried about. A narrowing of that gap, driven by the leaders coming back to the pack rather than the pack falling to the leaders, is the healthiest thing that could have happened.

QQQ versus IWM: small caps held while big tech broke, another vote for surgical not broad. QQQ fell 1.1%. IWM, the Russell 2000 small-cap index, fell just 0.5%. If Friday had been a genuine risk-off cascade, a real flight from equities driven by fear of a recession or a credit event, small caps would have been destroyed. Small caps are the highest-beta, most economically sensitive, least liquid corner of the equity market, and they get hit hardest and first when real fear takes over. Instead they outperformed big tech by a wide margin and lost only half a percent. That is impossible to square with a broad risk-off event. It is entirely consistent with a narrow unwind of crowded mega-cap positioning. The economically sensitive small caps were not where the borrowed money was sitting, so the small caps did not get hurt. The crowded tech was where the borrowed money was sitting, so the crowded tech got flushed.

Stocks versus bonds: bonds refused to bid, and that single fact rewrites the whole story. This is the tell I promised you, and it is the most important relationship on the board this week. When stocks fall on a genuine growth scare or recession fear, money flees into the safety of Treasuries. Bond prices rise, yields fall, and you see the classic risk-off rotation. That is the textbook flight to safety, and it is what a real top or a real growth panic produces every single time. This week, the opposite happened. Stocks fell and bonds fell with them. TLT, the long-dated Treasury proxy, dropped 0.8% on the week while the 10-year yield pushed above 4.5%. Bonds did not catch a safety bid. They sold off alongside equities. That tells you, unambiguously, that this was not a growth scare. Nobody was fleeing to safety, because the catalyst was not fear of a slowdown. The catalyst was a jobs report so hot it killed the rate-cut story and forced a repricing of rates higher, which hammered the most rate-sensitive, longest-duration, highest-multiple assets at once: long bonds and mega-cap growth. Stocks and bonds falling together is the signature of a rates-and-positioning shock, not a recession. And a rates shock that leaves the broad economy and the broad market intact is exactly the kind of dip I want to buy.

🚨 Sector Rotation

If you want to know whether investors were fleeing the market or shopping within it, watch where the money went, not just where it left. Money does not leave the market in a single afternoon and vanish. It rotates. And this week it rotated with a clear, deliberate, constructive logic that flatly contradicts the panic narrative.

Look at what went up on the week, in the same five sessions the headlines called a crash. Energy rose 3.6%. Health Care rose 3.1%. Financials rose 1.6%. Industrials rose 1.3%. Those are not defensive bunkers where terrified money hides waiting for the end. Energy and Financials and Industrials are economically sensitive, cyclically exposed, real-economy sectors. They are also, along with Health Care, the cheap, profitable, rate-insensitive parts of the market that get left behind in a momentum melt-up and get rediscovered when the froth comes off the leaders. Money flowed into them this week. That is the footprint of investors going shopping in the unloved, reasonably valued corners of the market, not the footprint of investors fleeing equities altogether. If this were the start of a true bear market, you would not see four sectors rally over 3% and 1% while the index fell. You would see everything red. Instead, the green was as broad as the red was narrow.

Now look at what went down. Technology fell 1.3%, and within it the semiconductors took the absolute worst of Friday's damage, which is the single biggest reason the Nasdaq printed its -4.2%. Communication Services fell 2.3%. Consumer Discretionary fell 2.6%. Biotech, the XBI, fell 2.7%. Notice the pattern. The losers are the high-multiple, high-momentum, crowded growth complex, the exact stuff that runs hottest in a melt-up and carries the most hot money. The semis, the AI-adjacent communication names, the speculative discretionary and biotech trades. These are the over-owned, over-loved, stretched-valuation corners. They got flushed. The cheap real-earnings sectors got bought. That is a rotation out of expensive momentum and into reasonable value, which is the most normal and healthy thing a market can do after a long one-directional run. It is shopping, and it is smart shopping.

💱 FX

Now the part where I stop making the bull case and tell you, honestly and without hedging my language, where I could be dead wrong. There is one real risk to everything I have written above, and it is not in the breadth, not in the vol surface, and not in the sectors. It is in the yen. USDJPY closed Friday at 160.2. It broke 160.

Let me explain why a single number on one currency pair can override a whole tape of healthy internals, because this is the one place I am not relaxed. The level 160 on dollar-yen is not arbitrary. It is the line where the Bank of Japan and the Ministry of Finance have historically stepped in to defend the yen, because a yen that falls too far too fast destabilizes Japan and ripples out through global funding markets. And they did defend it. Between late April and late May, Japan spent over ¥11.7 trillion, more than $73 billion, intervening to prop up the yen, its first intervention since 2024. That is an enormous sum of real money thrown directly at holding this line. And the yen has erased all of it. The same hot jobs print that hammered stocks on Friday widened the US-Japan interest-rate gap, because it pushed US rate-cut odds to zero and US rate-hike odds up, and that widening gap pulled the yen weaker, straight through the line that $73 billion was spent defending. The market ran the dollar right back through 160 in the face of the largest intervention in two years. That is power, and it demands respect.

Here is the mechanism that makes this matter for your portfolio even if you have never traded a currency in your life. The yen carry trade is one of the largest funding engines in global markets. For years, money has borrowed in yen at near-zero cost and invested the proceeds in higher-yielding assets all over the world, including US tech stocks. As long as the yen drifts weaker or stays stable, that trade prints money and the borrowed yen keeps flowing into risk assets. But the carry trade is short the yen, and a short position blows up when the thing you are short suddenly rallies. The danger is not the yen getting weaker. The danger is a violent snap-back the other way. If USDJPY breaks back under 155, that is where the carry unwind turns live, where the borrowed positioning that funded years of risk-taking gets forced to cover all at once, selling global assets to buy back yen in a self-reinforcing cascade. We have seen the rehearsal. August 2024, when a modest BoJ rate hike triggered a violent global unwind that took down equities worldwide in a matter of days. That was the rehearsal, and it was off a far smaller spark than the pressure building now.

So how do I hold both ideas at once, the constructive read on the flush and the genuine fear of the carry hinge? I hold them by sizing. This is the entire reason I am buying this dip slowly and selectively rather than backing up the truck with both hands. If the yen were sitting calmly at 150 with no intervention drama, I would be far more aggressive on the rotation, because the internal evidence is that good. But the yen is at 160.2, sitting on a broken line that $73 billion could not hold, with a live path to a carry unwind if it snaps back. That single fact caps my conviction and my position size. I am adding in the rate-insensitive, real-earnings parts of the market, the parts that got cheaper for no reason of their own, and I am doing it in measured pieces with cash held in reserve. The flush is my base case. The yen is my reason to stay humble about it, to keep dry powder, and to be ready to move the other way fast if 155 comes into view. Conviction in the thesis, humility about the one risk that could break it. That is the whole posture.

🧠 My Take

The crowd is loudest at the turn, and it is loudest in the wrong direction. That is the oldest pattern in this business. At real bottoms the consensus is certain the world is ending, and at real tops the consensus is certain the party never stops. Friday handed the consensus a clean, simple, emotionally satisfying story, the worst Nasdaq day in a year, the melt-up cracked, the top is in, and a thousand writers reached for it at once because it is easy and it feels smart to be bearish after a big red day. When a narrative is that unanimous and that easy, my job is to check whether the evidence actually supports it or whether everyone is just reading the same scary headline off the same index print. This week the evidence does not support the panic. The breadth held, the field stayed above its 50-day, the equal-weight index closed green, small caps barely moved, cheap real-earnings sectors rallied, and bonds fell with stocks instead of catching a safety bid. Every one of those facts cuts against "this is the top." So I am leaning into the rotation, with my eyes open and my size controlled.

Here are my two scenarios with rough probabilities, and I want to be clear that I am arguing the constructive case from the contrarian side, that the crowd has overshot to the bearish extreme, while still giving the cautious case the larger share because the yen is real.

Scenario one, Constructive on the rotation, about 45%. This is the flush-not-top case, and it is the one the internal evidence supports most directly. Friday was a positioning unwind in the crowded mega-cap AI complex, the borrowed money came out of the most over-owned trades, the froth got vented, and the broad market held throughout. In this scenario the rates shock gets digested over the next week or two, the VIX9D spike fades as front-end fear passes, the complacency ratio re-steepens back toward contango, and the rotation into cheap profitable sectors continues to broaden the market in a healthy way. The concentration that has worried me all year unwinds in an orderly fashion, the equal-weight catches up to the cap-weight rather than the reverse, and the index grinds back toward its highs on better internals than it had before. I am buying selectively into this, in the rate-insensitive, real-earnings names. The reason this is not my majority case is not the internals, which are excellent, but the yen. If 160 were not broken I would put this scenario well above 50%.

Scenario two, Cautious, about 55%. This is not the consensus top call. This is the carry-and-rates case, and it gets the larger share for one reason and one reason only, which is USDJPY at 160.2 sitting on a broken intervention line. In this scenario the rates shock is not a one-day event but the front edge of a sustained repricing higher, the 10-year keeps climbing past 4.5%, and the pressure on the yen builds until something forces the snap-back under 155 that turns the carry unwind live. If that happens, the borrowed money that funded years of global risk-taking gets pulled all at once, and a positioning unwind that started narrow in the AI names broadens into the kind of cross-asset deleveraging we saw in August 2024. In that world the healthy breadth I am leaning on this weekend breaks down fast, and the flush becomes the first leg of something larger. I hold this as the bigger probability out of respect for the yen, not out of agreement with the crowd that Friday alone proved a top. The crowd is right to be cautious and right for the wrong reason. The reason to be careful is the carry hinge, not the Nasdaq candle.

Notice what those numbers mean in practice. Constructive at 45% and Cautious at 55% is not a call to sell everything and hide, which is what the consensus is screaming. It is a call to participate carefully, to buy the dip in the safe corners with controlled size and real cash in reserve, and to stay nimble enough to flip defensive fast if the yen forces the issue. I am constructive on the rotation and humble about the hinge. That is a position you can act on without betting the account on being right.

🧘 The Philosopher's Corner

Seneca wrote that we suffer more often in imagination than in reality. I thought about that line all weekend as I watched a thousand versions of the same headline roll past, each one more certain than the last that the top was in and the end had begun. The imagination of the crowd ran far ahead of the reality of the tape. The reality was a single hard day in a handful of crowded names while the broad market stood its ground. The imagination was the apocalypse.

Marcus Aurelius had a discipline for exactly this moment. When something frightening came at him, he made a practice of stripping it down to what it actually was, separate from the story his mind wanted to wrap around it. Of looking at the thing plainly and asking what it really is, rather than what the fear says it is. That is the entire job on a weekend like this one. The scary headline says "worst Nasdaq day in a year, the top is in." Strip the story away and ask what the thing actually is, and the answer is narrower and quieter: a crowded trade unwound, the froth came off a few enormous names, and the median stock barely moved. The headline is the imagination. The breadth is the reality.

The crowd is loudest at the turn because the crowd trades its imagination, and the imagination is always more dramatic than the data. My only real edge, the only one worth having, is the willingness to do the unglamorous work of looking past the scary headline to ask what it actually hides. This weekend, what it hid was a market in better internal health than its index print suggested, rotating sensibly from the expensive to the cheap, with one genuine risk sitting quietly on a currency pair that almost nobody in the panic is even talking about. The crowd is screaming about the wrong thing and ignoring the right one. So I will keep asking the boring question, the one Seneca and Marcus would both recognize. Not "how does this feel," but "what is this, actually." The feeling says top. The thing itself says flush. I trade the thing itself.
