The Big Picture

Capital markets firms live and die by the deal cycle, and that cycle turned back up in 2026. Advisory fees, underwriting, and trading revenue all move together, and all three feed off the same thing: corporate confidence to raise money, buy rivals, and take risk. When that confidence returns, a diversified broker like Jefferies earns across every desk at once.

The cycle has been strong enough that the whole group is now stretched. Financial Services reads as tired here, late in its run rather than early. Money chasing the move is arriving after the easy gains, and a sector in that posture punishes anyone paying up for the obvious winner.

That backdrop cuts both ways for you. A revenue engine firing on the deal recovery is real and durable, and Jefferies posted 35% revenue growth over the trailing year to prove it. A sector already crowded and extended is the risk that any single stock in it grinds sideways while the group cools.

The setup that gets interesting inside a tired sector is a strong operator that has already pulled back hard. You want the earnings power without the crowd. Jefferies sits 22.6% below its 52-week high while its business is still growing, and that gap is where this piece lives.

Introducing $JEF

It runs two segments: Investment Banking and Capital Markets, and Asset Management. The first advises on mergers, underwrites debt and equity, syndicates loans, and runs the sales, trading, and prime brokerage desks. The second manages a group of alternative strategies across asset classes.

The firm was formerly Leucadia National Corporation and took the Jefferies name in May 2018. That history matters because it left the company with a broad balance sheet and a merchant-banking mindset on top of a pure Wall Street advisory business. You're buying a deal machine attached to a large book of financial assets.

The edge is scale without being a mega-bank. Jefferies is big enough to win real M&A and underwriting mandates, small enough to grow fast off a low base when the deal cycle turns. When advisory and underwriting reopen at the same time, a firm this size compounds quickly.
