📝 Essay notes · 53 Funds Built to Beat the Market. Almost None Beat Doing Nothing.

Idea minimalism from the latest essay: hook, building blocks, payload. Restack bait, zero product.
Note 1
Someone screenshotted every clever hedge fund still sold to the retail crowd.
Fifty three of them, lined up against just owning the index.
More than forty came out worse per unit of risk.
The pitch does not survive the receipt.
Note 2
The top of the leaderboard was a number a hedge fund would frame on the wall.
It also had 92 days of history.
A single quarter of being long the right thing is not a track record.
It is a screenshot with a lucky start date.
Note 3
The funds with a real track record back to the end of 2019 land in respectable territory.
Still behind the index they charge you like a hedge fund to trail.
Fees compound faster than edge in most quarters.
The receipt does not lie.
Note 4
Crash insurance funds have one job.
Cover the bad days without bleeding you on the good ones.
Almost none of them pull off the balance.
The insurance you pay for every year has to survive the years you do not need it.
Note 5
BTAL was built to rise when the market falls.
It compounded at negative 0.08 across the full window.
Doing its job on bad days is worthless when it bleeds on every other day.
That is the design flaw hidden inside the pitch.
Note 6
The clearest winner on the whole list was an ETF that owns the index with a tilt.
It barely hedges anything.
It charges almost nothing.
Sometimes the honest answer to a complicated question is a boring holding.
The morning boards make that easier to sit with.
