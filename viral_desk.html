Polymath 1
2985 stocks up, 2345 down, and QQQ closed 1.7 percent lower.

The index screen tells you one story. The count tells you another. Most names had a green day while a handful of the biggest weights dragged the tape.

The consensus tomorrow will call today a selloff. It was a rotation dressed as one. That distinction matters if you size off the index and hold the names underneath.

When breadth diverges from the headline, the count is usually right.

📝 Essay notes · 53 Funds Built to Beat the Market. Almost None Beat Doing Nothing.

Idea minimalism from the latest essay: hook, building blocks, payload. Restack bait, zero product.
Note 1
Someone graded 53 hedge funds against doing nothing.
Over 40 of them ran a negative information ratio.
The index paid better for less risk.
Paying up to trail what you were sold to beat is a career.
Note 2
Every alt fund gets sold on the same pitch.
Smoother ride. Crash insurance.
Then you check the up capture column.
Most of them killed it.
Low down capture does not save you when up capture is dead.
Note 3
The best returns on the list came from funds with 92 days of history.
Three months of luck reads exactly like skill.
The funds carrying the full 1610 days cluster far below.
Ask for the track length before you ask for the return.
Note 4
BTAL was designed to rise when the market falls.
Its CAGR came in at -8 percent per year for six years.
It did the job on the worst days and bled every other one.
Insurance you cannot afford to hold long enough to use is not insurance.
Note 5
SPMO was the single clean outperformer on the whole page.
An S&P momentum ETF.
Almost no fees, almost no hedge.
Just the index with a tilt.
The best alternative to the index turned out to be the index.
Note 6
Alts get graded in bull markets and it looks ugly.
Six years of melt-up is the hardest window for anything that hedges.
The bar is not whether they work in a crash.
It is whether they survive the up years so you own them when the down year comes.
