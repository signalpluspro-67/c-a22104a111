Opening — How long can eight stocks carry everything?

This is a market journal, not investment advice. Always size for the worst case before betting on the best one.

Ask ten traders this morning whether the market is healthy or stretched and you get ten different answers. The bull side points to breadth: three quarters of the eleven sectors above their fifty-day average, fresh new highs without matching new lows, financial stress in the bottom quartile of its multi-year range. The bear side will tell you that the high-beta to safe-asset ratio just hit the top one percent of its five-year history, that dealer positioning is buying protection sixty days out at sizes we have not seen in months, and that the eight largest mega-caps have outpaced the equal-weight index by twenty-five percentage points over sixty days. Both sides are correct. Both readings are true at the same time. That is the entire problem this week.

I have been doing this long enough to know that markets which look fine on top and stretched underneath rarely stay that way forever. One has to give. The question I have been turning over in my head all weekend is not whether the dispersion closes. It always closes. The question is whether the median catches up to the leaders, which would mean the rally broadens out and we get another six months of grinding higher, or whether the leaders catch down to the median, which would mean the eight stocks carrying the index finally take the breather their charts are begging for.

If you read this newsletter every week you know that I lean toward the second resolution this time. You also know that I am willing to be wrong about it. So this week I am defensive but I am not aggressive about being defensive. The difference matters. A trader who is defensive trims the leaders, hedges the book, and keeps dry powder for the rotation. A trader who is aggressive about being defensive shorts the index outright, declares a top, and ends up explaining the loss to himself for the next three months. I would rather be the first one. I have tried being the second one. It does not pay.

I should also say I might be wrong about this whole read. The macro signals on my dashboard are pointing one way and I have built a book around them, but the historical sample is six observations. I have learned to be humble about samples that small. What I am betting on this week is the asymmetry, not the certainty. If I am wrong, the cost is one or two percent on the year. If I am right, the position offsets a five to seven percent drawdown on the equity sleeve.

I keep going back and forth on this all weekend. The breadth side has the better optics, the macro side has the better history. I am leaning macro this week. I would not bet a year on it.

The point of writing a weekly newsletter on this stuff is not to be right about the macro every single week. The point is to be honest about what the data is telling me and to size positions in proportion to my conviction. The traders who blow up are not the ones who have wrong views. They are the ones who size their wrong views as if they were right views. The discipline is in the sizing, not in the conviction.

So when I tell you this week that I am defensive, what I really mean is that the macro signals on my dashboard are far enough into extreme territory that I am willing to take a small short bias and a small hedge cost in exchange for the optionality of being positioned for the historical resolution. If the rotation does not arrive, the cost of the position is one or two percent on the year. If the rotation does arrive, the position offsets a five to seven percent drawdown on the equity sleeve. That is the asymmetry I am paying for. The asymmetry is real even if the timing is uncertain.

Why this rally has merit (the bull case I take seriously)

Let me start with the side I lean against, because the bull case is real and any argument that pretends otherwise is dishonest. The companies driving this rally are not the speculative shells of 2000. They are some of the most profitable enterprises in the history of capitalism. Their cash flows are growing. Their margins are expanding. The technology they sell is genuinely changing how people work, and the productivity gains being attributed to artificial intelligence are starting to show up in the actual operating numbers, not just in the slide decks.

The other thing the bulls have right is that the broad market is not as stretched as the financial press says it is. If you strip out the eight largest mega-caps and look at the equal-weight index, you find a market that has barely participated in the rally. The median stock is up less than one percent over the past sixty days. That is not a euphoric tape. By that measure, the broad market is actually undervalued relative to its own recent history, and a rotation that brings the median up to meet the leaders would be a profitable thing to be positioned for.

So why am I not in the bull camp this week? Because the historical resolution to this kind of dispersion is rarely the median catching up. The historical resolution is the leaders catching down. The reason is mechanical. Passive flows and momentum strategies create a self-reinforcing loop. Index funds buy more of what is already big. Momentum strategies buy more of what is already going up. The bigger and more up a stock gets, the more mechanical buying it attracts. That loop continues until something breaks the inflow or until the underlying earnings stop justifying the multiple expansion. When the loop breaks, the same mechanics work in reverse. Passive funds sell what gets smaller. Momentum strategies sell what stops going up. The leaders catch down faster than the median can catch up.

Zoetis — the long anecdote

I want to spend a few paragraphs on Zoetis specifically because it is the trade I am most confident about for this week, and the path that brought me to it illustrates how I think about position selection in a defensive macro setup.

Zoetis is the global leader in animal health. The company spun out of Pfizer in 2013 and has been one of the most consistent compounders in the healthcare sector since. The business is straightforward. They sell vaccines, diagnostics, and medications for pets and livestock. The pet side has been the growth engine, driven by the long secular trend of pet ownership in developed markets. The livestock side is mature and stable, providing a consistent cash flow base.

Over the past four months the stock has been hammered. The narrative driving the drawdown is that pet ownership growth is decelerating in the post-COVID hangover, that consumer spending on vet care is softening, and that the company will need to guide down on the next earnings call. The stock is now down more than thirty percent from its prior high, the fifty-day average is rolling over, and the relative strength index just hit six point four. That is the lowest reading I have seen on a quality large-cap in this cycle. It is a once-a-cycle event.

That gap between price and reality is what creates the opportunity. If you only had bandwidth for one trade this week, this is the one I would choose. For traders who prefer options to equity, this is the kind of setup where a long-dated call can be more efficient than the equity itself.
