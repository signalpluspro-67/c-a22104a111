The Tape Is Splitting and Nobody's Talking About It

Wednesday, July 1 2026

The Saturday thesis holds. We flagged rotation pressure building under the surface, and Monday's tape confirmed it with a clean divergence: QQQ down -1.4% while small caps barely flinched at -0.3%. The broad market is softening, but the character of the selling matters more than the headline number.

What the split tells us

SPY closed Monday at 742.67, down half a percent. Fine. Normal. But look at where the damage landed. The Nasdaq 100 ate nearly three times the loss of the Russell 2000. That ratio, QQQ versus IWM, compressed by over a full percentage point in a single session. When mega-cap tech drops -1.4% and small caps lose -0.3%, money isn't leaving the building. It's changing rooms.

The VIX confirms this read. It fell -2.9% to 15.98 on a down day. That's not how fear works. If this were the start of a real risk-off move, you'd see the VIX climbing alongside the selling. Instead it's fading. The options market is telling you this is orderly distribution out of crowded large-cap names, not a liquidation event.

I've seen this pattern before. Late June, early July. Funds rebalance. The quarter just ended. Performance chasers take profit in winners and redeploy into laggards. The flows look scary on the QQQ chart but they're mechanical, not emotional.

That's the setup for the three names on our desk right now.

Bottom plays in a rotation tape

All three fresh setups, CPB, RNGR, and ACAD, are long entries on names that have been beaten down and are showing signs of basing. This is the kind of tape where these trades work.

CPB at $23.20 is the one I keep coming back to. Campbell's has short squeeze confluence layered on top of a bottom formation. The buy zone sits right here, with a target of $25.48 and a stop at $21.67. That's roughly a 2.5:1 reward-to-risk ratio on a consumer staples name during a rotation into defensives. The sector tailwind matters. When tech leads the downside, staples tend to catch a bid from the same rebalancing flows I described above.

RNGR at $16.07 has a moving average crossover adding momentum to the bottom setup. Target $17.48, stop $15.14. Tighter trade, about 1.5:1, but the MA cross gives you a technical trigger that most bottom-fishing entries lack. You want confirmation before you commit capital to a name that's been in the gutter, and a cross gives you that.

ACAD at $25.15 is the most interesting of the three from a squeeze perspective. Biotech names with short squeeze signals during broad market rotation can move fast. Target $27.90, stop $23.32. The risk here is binary: biotech can gap on headlines regardless of technicals. Size accordingly.

What connects all three is the setup type. These are not momentum chases. They're contrarian entries in beaten-down names where the tape is starting to cooperate. Rotation out of mega-cap tech creates exactly the kind of environment where small and mid-cap bottoms get traction. Money has to go somewhere.

At that point, the long setups still work (staples and healthcare hold up in defensive tapes too), but you'd want to tighten stops and reduce overall exposure.

Two things. First, the QQQ/IWM ratio. If small caps continue to outperform large caps by this margin through Wednesday and Thursday, the rotation is real and has legs. That's the green light for the desk setups above. Second, VIX 16. A move above 16 on a closing basis would signal that the selling is broadening from rebalancing into genuine risk reduction. Below 16, this remains a reshuffling of chairs.

The tape is telling a clear story if you're willing to listen. The headline says "stocks down." The internals say "stocks moving." Those are different things, and they call for different responses. Headline readers will hedge. Rotation readers will redeploy. You know which camp I'm in.

Stay patient. The setups are there. Let the tape come to you.
