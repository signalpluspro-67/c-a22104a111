The Flush Held. Now the Bank of Japan Gets a Vote.

Market Recap, June 13th 2026

Hello my friends,

Last week I made an unpopular call. While your feed screamed "top" after the June 5 flush, I told you it was a positioning flush rather than a market top, and that two things would decide whether I was right: the breadth had to hold, and the yen was the one variable that could override everything else. One week later, I have my answer on the first, and a date on the second.

The breadth held. It did better than hold, it widened. SPY finished the week almost exactly flat at $738.37, up a rounding error from the prior Friday, and underneath that quiet headline the average stock beat the index for a second straight week. The market passed the test I laid out.

The yen did not resolve. USDJPY sat at 160.2 all week, glued to the line that $73 billion of intervention could not hold, and the Bank of Japan meets next week with a rate hike on the table. So the risk I flagged did not fade. It acquired a date.

Today's edition: what the internals confirmed, and why I am still keeping powder dry into a central bank meeting.

The headline index went nowhere. SPY closed the week at $738.37, up roughly 0.1% from the prior Friday. That flat print hides the real story, which is that the broad market kept healing while the giants rested.

The clearest read is the share of stocks holding their intermediate uptrend. Going into the week, 53.1% of the liquid market traded above its 50-day moving average. By Friday that share had climbed to 58.7%. The index stalled and the participation underneath it grew. That is the opposite of how a top behaves.

The retest came Wednesday. SPY dipped to about $725 intraday, the low of the week, then bought back over the next two sessions to close green for the week. A real top does not repurchase its retest in forty-eight hours. Friday's tape backed it up, with advancers beating decliners better than two to one and new highs outnumbering new lows.

Why this still reads as a flush

A real top builds over weeks of quiet internal decay, breadth falling away from price long before the index gives in. What we got was the inverse twice running: price took the hit on June 5, and the breadth refused to follow, then improved while the index sat still. Last week's flush-not-top call now has a full week of confirming internals behind it.

If Market Health tells you what the market did, volatility tells you how it felt doing it. Every gauge this week says the same thing: the scare got absorbed.

VIX peaked at 22.2 on Wednesday's dip and settled back to 17.68 by Friday. The 9-day gauge, which had nearly doubled into the June 5 flush, collapsed from 23.9 to 17.3, so the very short-term panic fully drained. It now sits a touch below the 30-day VIX, the calm-market configuration.

The complacency ratio I track, VIX3M over VIX, re-steepened from its 1.01 panic low back to 1.16. The market rebuilt its layer of calm rather than spiraling lower, which is the rebound base case I laid out last week.

One gauge keeps me honest. SKEW, the price of crash insurance, eased from 152 to 142.6, lower but still elevated. The professional bid for downside protection cooled, yet it never went away. That standing bid is the market quietly pricing the same tail I am pricing, and that tail has a name and a date this week.

Last week the pairs told a clean story of a rates shock hitting long duration. This week they tell the recovery half of the same story, and every pair that broke against the bulls mended.

  • The flushed growth bounced hardest. Nasdaq rose 1.24% against the Dow's 0.52%. The complex that took the June 5 hit led the rebound, which is how a positioning flush reverses.
  • Breadth beat the giants again. Equal-weight gained 1.36% while the cap-weighted index added 0.1%. The market broadened for a second week.
  • Small caps led everything at +3.64%. The most rate-sensitive corner, hit hardest in the flush, rebounded the most as the 10-year eased from 4.54% back to 4.49%. The rate impulse that caused the damage is unwinding at the margin.
  • Stocks and bonds stopped falling together. Long Treasuries firmed 0.6% as yields slipped. The rates shock is cooling rather than compounding.

To know whether money was fleeing or shopping, you watch where it went. This week it rotated back toward exactly what got flushed.

Biotech, down nearly 6% in last week's flush, led the rebound at +3.7%. Materials and Staples each added 2.7%, Financials 1.4%. Energy, last week's safe-haven winner, was the only sector to finish red, down 0.8%, as money rotated out of the one place it had hidden. Eleven of twelve sectors closed green.

A market-wide liquidation does not paint eleven sectors green the week after it strikes. This is digestion, and it is broad. The concentration I have warned about for months keeps unwinding the healthy way, with the laggards catching up rather than the leaders breaking down.

Here is the part that keeps my conviction capped, the same one as last week, only now it has a clock on it.

The yen did not move. USDJPY sat at 160.2 all week, still above the line that more than $73 billion of intervention failed to defend. A stuck price is not news on its own. The news is the calendar. The Bank of Japan meets next week, and the market expects its first rate hike since December.

A BoJ hike is precisely the spark that can turn the carry unwind live. Higher Japanese rates narrow the gap that funds the carry trade, and if the yen finally snaps back toward 155, the borrowed positioning that has financed years of global risk-taking gets forced to cover at once, selling assets worldwide to buy back yen. We saw the rehearsal in August 2024, off a smaller spark than the one sitting on next week's calendar.

So the single most important event for your book next week is not a US data point. It is a central bank meeting in Tokyo.

🧠 My Take

The internals won the argument this week. Breadth widened, volatility drained, the flushed sectors led the bounce, and last week's read held up cleanly. By the data alone I should be leaning harder into the rotation.

I am holding back, and the reason is the calendar rather than the tape. The yen risk did not fade, it got a date. So I am moving to roughly 50/50, and I am keeping cash in reserve specifically into the BoJ decision.

Constructive (~50%)

The flush-not-top case, now with a full week of confirming breadth behind it. The rates shock keeps getting digested, small caps keep healing as yields ease, and the rotation broadens the market while the index grinds back toward its highs on better internals than before. If next week were quiet, this would be well above half.

Cautious (~50%)

The carry hinge. A BoJ hike snaps the yen back, the August 2024 unwind plays out in miniature, and a narrow flush in the crowded names broadens into cross-asset deleveraging. The crowd is right to be careful, for the wrong reason. The reason is Tokyo, not the breadth.

In practice: I stay in the rotation I already own, I do not chase the bounce into the meeting, and I am ready to flip defensive fast if the yen forces it. Participate, do not lunge.
