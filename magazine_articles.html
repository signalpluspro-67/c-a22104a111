The 7% Rule That Keeps Traders Alive

+----------------------------------------------------------+
|       7% RISK PER TRADE: WHY THE CAP MATTERS             |
|                                                          |
| Account size: $10,000        Outcome after 10 losses     |
|                                                          |
|   2%   ##########            $8,171  (annoying)          |
|   5%   ########              $5,987  (bruised)           |
|   7%   #######               $4,840  (Rosenstock cap)    |
|  15%   ##                    $1,969  (near broken)       |
|  25%   #                     $  563  (ruined)            |
|                                                          |
| Small fixed risk turns ugly streaks into survivable      |
| events. Big risk turns them into account funerals.       |
+----------------------------------------------------------+

Forty years ago, John Rosenstock published a piece in this magazine's first issue called "Integrated Systems Approach to Technical Commodity Analysis." Most of what he wrote about Sibbet-Hadady charts and Apple II terminals reads like an antique today. One paragraph still pays rent. He laid out two arithmetic rules for sizing positions, and those rules keep traders alive whether they are trading pork bellies on a green screen or selling weekly SPY puts on a phone.

The rules are short. Risk no more than 7% of your account on any single trade. Carry no more than 5x the account's cash in open notional exposure. Everything else in this article is the math and the muscle behind those two numbers.

Why probability of ruin matters more than probability of being right

Rosenstock borrowed the phrase "probability of ruin" from Richard Teweles, who wrote The Commodity Futures Game in the 1970s. The idea predates computers and survives every regime. A coin pays 2:1 on heads. The expected value looks excellent. If each flip costs you 50% of your stack, you go broke before the law of large numbers shows up. Edge buys you nothing if variance kills you first.

The trader's job is to make the loss per trade small enough that ten ugly trades in a row leave you with capital to keep playing. That is what the 7% cap delivers. It buys time for your method, your screener, and your judgment to assert themselves.

What 7% per trade does to a losing streak

Run the compounding. Start with $10,000. Lose ten trades in a row at fixed fractional risk:

  • At 2% per trade you finish with $8,171. Annoying.
  • At 5% per trade you finish with $5,987. Bruised.
  • At 7% per trade you finish with $4,840. The Rosenstock ceiling.
  • At 15% per trade you finish with $1,969. Near broken.
  • At 25% per trade you finish with $563. Ruined.

Notice the cliff between 5% and 15% per trade. Below the cliff, the account remains a working tool. Above it, the account becomes a recreational hobby. A 50% drawdown needs a 100% gain to break even. A 90% drawdown needs a 900% gain. The 7% rule keeps you in the part of the table where the math is on your side.

How to translate 7% into a stop distance on a real ticker

The 7% number measures account-level risk. Apply it to your total account, then back into the stop distance and the share count.

Say your account is $30,000. Your maximum loss on the next trade is $2,100. You want to buy NVDA at $180 with a technical stop at $168. The per-share risk is $12. Divide: $2,100 divided by $12 gives 175 shares. That is your maximum size. If conviction is lower, take half. Never more.

The arithmetic forces honesty. A wide stop on a volatile name shrinks the position automatically. A tight stop on a quiet name lets you size up while still respecting the account cap. The stop and the size are decided together, before entry, never after.

The 5x exposure cap, translated for modern traders

Rosenstock's second rule said an account should never control positions worth more than 5x its cash. He was thinking about futures, where a single cattle contract represents $25,000 of underlying meat on $2,500 of margin. The principle ports to anything margined: options notional, crypto perpetuals, levered ETFs.

For a $30,000 account, your total open notional across all positions stays at $150,000 or less. That sounds restrictive until you watch what happens in a gap. A 10% opening gap against you on 5x notional is a 50% account hit. On 10x, the account is gone. The cap is what stops a single overnight headline from ending a career.

The trap of "this one is different"

Every trader has a story about the trade they sized up because the setup looked too clean to skip. Sometimes that trade pays off and the trader learns the wrong lesson. The discipline gets relaxed on the next high-conviction setup. The fourth or fifth time, the gap goes the other way and the account exits the business.

The 7% and 5x rules work because they apply when you feel certain. Caution sizes itself. The skill is refusing the upsize when the conviction is loud.

Where the rules fall short

The 7% cap assumes uncorrelated trades. Put on five long tech names at 7% each and you do not have five independent risks. You have one 35% risk on the tech factor. A NASDAQ gap-down liquidates the whole basket together. The honest version of the rule treats a correlated basket as one position for the cap.

The 5x notional limit also leaks if you stack options. A long call has limited dollar downside, but notional through delta can be misleading. Many traders track delta-adjusted exposure as a sanity check against the gross figure.

Both rules say nothing about win rate or expectancy. They are survival rules. They will not produce edge on their own. You still need a method that wins enough to cover slippage and commissions. The rules give that method room to breathe.

How to apply this Monday morning

  • Write your account size at the top of your trading log.
  • Compute 7% of it. That dollar figure is your maximum loss per trade.
  • Compute 5x of it. That dollar figure is your maximum gross notional across all open positions.
  • For each new trade, set the stop first, then size from the stop. Never the other way.
  • Treat correlated names as one position. A basket of regional banks counts as one trade for the cap.

Rosenstock wrote in a vacuum tube era. The hardware in your pocket is faster than the mainframe he was learning to escape. The arithmetic of survival has not aged a day.

Key takeaways
  • Risk no more than 7% of account equity on any single trade.
  • Carry no more than 5x account cash in total open notional exposure.
  • Set the stop first, then size shares from the stop. Never reverse.
  • Correlated positions count as one trade against the 7% cap.
  • Survival rules buy time for your edge. They do not create edge.
