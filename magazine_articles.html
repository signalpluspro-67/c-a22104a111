The 1×1 Filter: How Gann's Simplest Tool Teaches a Modern Trader to Stay on the Right Side

+------------------------------------------------------------+
| GANN 1x1 REGIME FILTER: WHICH SIDE OF THE LINE?            |
|                                                            |
| price                                                      |
|   ^                                                        |
|   |                          .....x  (close above 1x1)     |
|   |                      ....                              |
|   |                  ....                                  |
|   |              ..../  <- 1x1 from swing LOW (BULL line)  |
|   |          ....                                          |
|   |      ....                                              |
|   |  ../                                                   |
|   | x  (swing low pivot, confirmed by 3 higher highs)      |
|   +------------------------------------------------> time  |
|                                                            |
|   Above the bull 1x1 = trade longs. Skip shorts.           |
|   Close back below   = regime flips. Hands off longs.      |
|                                                            |
| Takeaway: regime beats entry. Pick the right half          |
| of the chart first, then let your signals do their job.    |
+------------------------------------------------------------+

W.D. Gann left behind a sprawling library of methods. Most of them are too elaborate for a working trader to apply consistently. One tool from his kit survives every passing era and every change in market structure: the 1×1 angle, the line that moves one unit of price for one unit of time. Curtis Arnold's 1982 piece in Technical Analysis of Stocks & Commodities (Vol. 1 No. 3) paired this angle with Gann's swing-bottom and swing-top rules, then walked through a T-bond test that produced 9 winners and 5 losers, with a 55% annualized return on a properly capitalized account. Jesse Thompson's gold article in the same issue and Jack Hutson's cotton trade hammer the same point from different angles: the trader who knows the side of the dominant trend wins more than the trader who knows the perfect entry.

The idea in one sentence

Draw a line from the last confirmed swing pivot at a fixed slope. As long as price holds above the line drawn from a low, you're in a bull regime. As long as price holds below the line drawn from a high, you're in a bear regime. A clean close on the wrong side flips the regime.

That is the entire machine. The discipline lives inside two questions: what counts as a swing pivot, and what counts as a clean close.

Why a 45 degree line is not arbitrary

Gann's 1×1 assumes price and time deserve the same weight. On a properly scaled chart it splits the plane into two halves. Above the line, buyers are paying up faster than time is passing. Below it, sellers are. The angle is a crude trend-strength filter that costs nothing to compute and never repaints.

Arnold's swing rule keeps the pivots honest. A swing low only confirms after 3 days of higher highs, with one exception: if day three is a reversal day (price pushes higher then closes near the low, below the midpoint, below yesterday's close), you wait for day four. That single rule prevents the trader from anchoring a trendline on a hook that the market is about to punish.

What the test actually showed

Arnold's T-bond walk-through covered roughly 1.6 years and produced 14 round-trips. The largest gain was $5,152. The largest single loss was $2,472. Peak drawdown was $2,569, about 18% of a properly funded $14,000 account. Net profit after commissions: $12,323.

The win rate was modest, around 64%, and the system spent long stretches getting chopped in sideways tape. What saved it was the asymmetry. Losses were capped by the next swing pivot. Winners ran until price closed back through a 1×1 from the opposite pivot. The system gave back the first and last leg of every move. In exchange, it never missed the middle.

The limits, stated plainly

Three weaknesses show up the moment you use this in practice.

  • Chop tax. In range-bound markets the 1×1 generates a string of small losses. Arnold's T-bond test had a stretch of three whipsaws in roughly six weeks.
  • Scale sensitivity. A 45 degree line depends on how you scale the chart. The slope must be defined in price units per time unit, not visually. On a daily chart of a $100 stock, a 1×1 that moves $1 per day is reasonable. On a $10 stock, you'd use $0.10 per day.
  • Late exits. The exit signal is the same as the entry rule, which means you always give back a piece of the move. Arnold's October trade caught a sharp rally and surrendered most of it before the close-through fired.

The fix Thompson offers in the same issue is to define the dominant trend on a slower frame first. His monthly swing chart on gold marks higher swing lows as a bull regime and lower swing highs as a bear regime, with a $10 buffer to filter noise. When the dominant trend is up, you trade the daily 1×1 long signals and skip the shorts. The chop tax drops sharply.

How a working trader applies this in 2026

The 1×1 idea translates into three rules that fit a modern setup notebook.

  • Define regime on a slow frame. Weekly or monthly swing pivots are enough. If the last confirmed swing pivot is a higher low and price holds above a slow trend filter, you take only long setups.
  • Use a fixed-slope trendline from the last daily pivot. Pick a slope that's a fraction of the instrument's average true range, say 0.25 ATR per day. Above the line, your long setups have the wind at their back. Below it, treat the same setups with suspicion.
  • Confirm pivots with a reversal-day check. Three closes in the new direction, unless day three is a reversal. This single filter saves the trader from anchoring to fakeouts.

The deeper lesson across all three 1982 articles is that regime beats entry. Hutson's cotton trade was triggered by a stochastic divergence and an RSI divergence, but the real edge came from being long during a confirmed bull leg. Arnold's T-bond system won by refusing to fight the dominant slope. Thompson's gold chart said the same thing in capital letters: when the monthly swing flips from down to up, the next 6 to 12 months tend to reward longs and punish shorts.

How it fits a modern workflow

In a screener-and-confluence process, a 1×1 style regime filter belongs at the top of the funnel, before any signal scoring. Use a slow trend filter (price versus a long moving average, or a weekly swing pivot rule) to decide which side of the book is active that week, then let your confluence scorers pick the best names on that side. Position size scales with the trade's distance to the next swing pivot, which gives you a built-in risk-first stop. The 1×1 doesn't pick winners. It tells you which half of the chart your winners are hiding in.

Key takeaways
  • Gann's 1x1 angle is a regime filter, not an entry signal: it tells you which side of the book is alive.
  • A swing pivot only counts after 3 closes in the new direction, with a reversal-day exception on day 3.
  • Arnold's T-bond test produced 9 winners and 5 losers, +55% annualized, with -18% peak drawdown.
  • The system always gives back the first and last leg of a move; the trade-off is you never miss the middle.
  • Confirm the dominant trend on a slower frame (weekly or monthly swing) to cut the chop tax in sideways tape.

Three Bands, Three Jobs: Splitting Price Into Trend, Cycle, and Noise

+----------------------------------------------------------+
| PRICE = TREND + CYCLE + NOISE   3 BANDS, 3 JOBS          |
|                                                          |
| LOW  (>60d)    regime    ____----''''----____            |
|                                                          |
| MID  (6-60d)   setup     _/\__/\__/\__/\__/\_            |
|                                                          |
| HIGH (<6d)     trigger   /\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\            |
|                                                          |
| Stop sized to your trigger band.                         |
| Holding period sized to your setup band.                 |
| Playbook chosen by your regime band.                     |
+----------------------------------------------------------+

Most price series you look at hide three things stacked on top of each other. A slow drift. A few medium swings. A lot of daily wiggle. If you trade them as one signal, you guess. If you split them, you pick the right tool for each layer.

That insight is 45 years old in trading circles. The January 1983 issue of Stocks & Commodities laid it out in plain terms across three pieces: Anthony Warren's A MiniGuide to Fourier Spectrum Analysis, Jack Hutson's Using Fourier, and the magazine's review article Moving Averages. Eric Weiss made the same point from a different angle in ARIMA Forecasting when he insisted you difference a series before modelling it, so the trend stops contaminating the cycle.

The vocabulary has changed. The idea has held.

Three bands, three jobs

Warren split the spectrum of daily price data into three buckets and gave each a use.

The low frequency band held cycles longer than 60 days. Seasonal drift, long term trend, regime. The intermediate band held the 6 to 60 day swings, where most discretionary trade decisions live. The high frequency band, anything under 6 days, was mostly noise. He wrote that most of the spectrum sits in that high band. That sentence is the one to tape on the inside of your screen.

If most of what you see day to day is noise, then most of what reacts to it is noise too. You too.

Why it matters now

You probably do not run a Fast Fourier Transform on soybean oil. You also do not need to. The lesson survives the tooling.

When you build a screener pipeline, every signal you stack belongs to one of those three bands. An SMA200 filter and a sector rotation read are low frequency calls. A pullback to bb_pctb 0.3 with a fresh BOTTOM print is intermediate. A 15 minute opening range breakout is high frequency. They answer different questions. Mixing them without saying which is which is how confluence becomes confusion.

The same idea explains why the question what is the right moving average length never had a clean answer. The 1983 review piece admitted as much, saying each instrument tends to have its own characteristic number and that the number drifts over time. The right N depends on which band you want to keep. A 5 day average passes the high frequency wiggle through. A 200 day average smooths it out and leaves you with regime. Choose the band first. Choose the window after.

What ARIMA quietly told you

Weiss spent half his article on differencing. Subtract yesterday from today. Look at the residual. If the trend was a clean +5 points per day, the residual sits near 0 with normal noise around it. The model then works on the cleaner residual and adds the trend back at the end.

That is the same move as the spectrum split. Pull the slow component out. Trade the faster one against a stable baseline. The 2026 version of that workflow is detrending against SPY, computing excess returns, scoring screeners on the residual, and ranking trades on the cleaned series. Every honest backtest in your stack already does some version of this. The 1983 article only named it.

Where the lesson breaks

Two limits to keep in mind.

First, gaps and noise distort the picture. Warren warned that long data intervals of at least 64 observations are needed for the spectrum to resolve anything. Modern equivalent: do not run a cycle read on 20 bars and call it a signal. You will get a peak. It will be an artifact.

Second, the bands shift. The characteristic number Hutson described moves with regime. A 5 day mean reversion edge in calm tape becomes a 2 day edge in stress and a zero edge in mega cap dispersion. So you re run the spectrum, or you re calibrate the moving average, or you accept that the band has closed and rotate. The decomposition is a recurring exercise, never a fixed setting.

Putting it on a screen

The cleanest version of this for a working trader looks like a triage.

Pull the daily close. Detrend with a long moving average or against an index. Look at the residual. Ask three questions in order. Is the trend up or down across 60 days or more, and what does that say about which playbook is allowed today. Are there clean swings of 2 to 8 weeks visible in the residual, and where am I inside one of them. What is the noise envelope of the last 5 days, and is my stop wider than that envelope.

You can do all three with one chart and one ATR read. You do not need a Fourier transform. You need the discipline to say this entry is a band 2 trade, my stop is sized for band 3, my exit comes from band 1 instead of stacking unrelated signals and calling the result confluence.

Two practical traps

  • A screener that fires on noise will fire often. A high win rate count means little if every trade is band 3 and your stop is band 2 wide. Check the holding period and the average move size before you trust the win rate.
  • A regime filter that runs on band 1 cannot save a band 3 entry. The SPY > SMA200 gate from the BREAKOUT V7 work is a good example. It removes obviously hostile tape. It does not tell you the breakout will hold for more than 3 bars. Different bands, different jobs.

How it fits a modern workflow

Confluence reads better when every screener you stack is labelled by band. A regime gate (low frequency), a setup screener (intermediate), and a trigger (high frequency) give you three independent yeses instead of three correlated ones. Position sizing then follows the slowest band on the chart, since that is the one that sets how long you intend to hold and how much room the trade needs to breathe. The 1983 magazine called this spectrum analysis. You can call it triage. The work is the same.

Key takeaways
  • Price hides three layers: regime (>60d), swing (6-60d), noise (<6d).
  • Most daily action is noise. Most reactions to it are noise too.
  • Every screener belongs to one band. Label it before you stack it.
  • Stop size comes from the trigger band, hold time from the setup band.
  • Detrending and differencing are the same move under different names.

The 7% Rule That Keeps Traders Alive

+----------------------------------------------------------+
|       7% RISK PER TRADE: WHY THE CAP MATTERS             |
|                                                          |
| Account size: $10,000        Outcome after 10 losses     |
|                                                          |
|   2%   ##########            $8,171  (annoying)          |
|   5%   ########              $5,987  (bruised)           |
|   7%   #######               $4,840  (Rosenstock cap)    |
|  15%   ##                    $1,969  (near broken)       |
|  25%   #                     $  563  (ruined)            |
|                                                          |
| Small fixed risk turns ugly streaks into survivable      |
| events. Big risk turns them into account funerals.       |
+----------------------------------------------------------+

Forty years ago, John Rosenstock published a piece in this magazine's first issue called "Integrated Systems Approach to Technical Commodity Analysis." Most of what he wrote about Sibbet-Hadady charts and Apple II terminals reads like an antique today. One paragraph still pays rent. He laid out two arithmetic rules for sizing positions, and those rules keep traders alive whether they are trading pork bellies on a green screen or selling weekly SPY puts on a phone.

The rules are short. Risk no more than 7% of your account on any single trade. Carry no more than 5x the account's cash in open notional exposure. Everything else in this article is the math and the muscle behind those two numbers.

Why probability of ruin matters more than probability of being right

Rosenstock borrowed the phrase "probability of ruin" from Richard Teweles, who wrote The Commodity Futures Game in the 1970s. The idea predates computers and survives every regime. A coin pays 2:1 on heads. The expected value looks excellent. If each flip costs you 50% of your stack, you go broke before the law of large numbers shows up. Edge buys you nothing if variance kills you first.

The trader's job is to make the loss per trade small enough that ten ugly trades in a row leave you with capital to keep playing. That is what the 7% cap delivers. It buys time for your method, your screener, and your judgment to assert themselves.

What 7% per trade does to a losing streak

Run the compounding. Start with $10,000. Lose ten trades in a row at fixed fractional risk:

  • At 2% per trade you finish with $8,171. Annoying.
  • At 5% per trade you finish with $5,987. Bruised.
  • At 7% per trade you finish with $4,840. The Rosenstock ceiling.
  • At 15% per trade you finish with $1,969. Near broken.
  • At 25% per trade you finish with $563. Ruined.

Notice the cliff between 5% and 15% per trade. Below the cliff, the account remains a working tool. Above it, the account becomes a recreational hobby. A 50% drawdown needs a 100% gain to break even. A 90% drawdown needs a 900% gain. The 7% rule keeps you in the part of the table where the math is on your side.

How to translate 7% into a stop distance on a real ticker

The 7% number measures account-level risk. Apply it to your total account, then back into the stop distance and the share count.

Say your account is $30,000. Your maximum loss on the next trade is $2,100. You want to buy NVDA at $180 with a technical stop at $168. The per-share risk is $12. Divide: $2,100 divided by $12 gives 175 shares. That is your maximum size. If conviction is lower, take half. Never more.

The arithmetic forces honesty. A wide stop on a volatile name shrinks the position automatically. A tight stop on a quiet name lets you size up while still respecting the account cap. The stop and the size are decided together, before entry, never after.

The 5x exposure cap, translated for modern traders

Rosenstock's second rule said an account should never control positions worth more than 5x its cash. He was thinking about futures, where a single cattle contract represents $25,000 of underlying meat on $2,500 of margin. The principle ports to anything margined: options notional, crypto perpetuals, levered ETFs.

For a $30,000 account, your total open notional across all positions stays at $150,000 or less. That sounds restrictive until you watch what happens in a gap. A 10% opening gap against you on 5x notional is a 50% account hit. On 10x, the account is gone. The cap is what stops a single overnight headline from ending a career.

The trap of "this one is different"

Every trader has a story about the trade they sized up because the setup looked too clean to skip. Sometimes that trade pays off and the trader learns the wrong lesson. The discipline gets relaxed on the next high-conviction setup. The fourth or fifth time, the gap goes the other way and the account exits the business.

The 7% and 5x rules work because they apply when you feel certain. Caution sizes itself. The skill is refusing the upsize when the conviction is loud.

Where the rules fall short

The 7% cap assumes uncorrelated trades. Put on five long tech names at 7% each and you do not have five independent risks. You have one 35% risk on the tech factor. A NASDAQ gap-down liquidates the whole basket together. The honest version of the rule treats a correlated basket as one position for the cap.

The 5x notional limit also leaks if you stack options. A long call has limited dollar downside, but notional through delta can be misleading. Many traders track delta-adjusted exposure as a sanity check against the gross figure.

Both rules say nothing about win rate or expectancy. They are survival rules. They will not produce edge on their own. You still need a method that wins enough to cover slippage and commissions. The rules give that method room to breathe.

How to apply this Monday morning

  • Write your account size at the top of your trading log.
  • Compute 7% of it. That dollar figure is your maximum loss per trade.
  • Compute 5x of it. That dollar figure is your maximum gross notional across all open positions.
  • For each new trade, set the stop first, then size from the stop. Never the other way.
  • Treat correlated names as one position. A basket of regional banks counts as one trade for the cap.

Rosenstock wrote in a vacuum tube era. The hardware in your pocket is faster than the mainframe he was learning to escape. The arithmetic of survival has not aged a day.

Key takeaways
  • Risk no more than 7% of account equity on any single trade.
  • Carry no more than 5x account cash in total open notional exposure.
  • Set the stop first, then size shares from the stop. Never reverse.
  • Correlated positions count as one trade against the 7% cap.
  • Survival rules buy time for your edge. They do not create edge.
