What the macro tape is telling you

The tension is this: credit markets are pricing a world where everything is fine, while the real economy data is quietly flashing amber. You have HY OAS at essentially **3 bps**, financial stress indexes deeply negative (St. Louis FSI at **-0.69**, Chicago FCI at **-0.49**), and a yield curve that has re-steepened into positive territory — **+41 bps** on the 2s10s, **+71 bps** on the 3m10s. Credit is not worried. Credit is practically asleep. And yet the macro regime score sits at **10/100**, the lowest tier, driven by two structural forces that don't care what credit thinks: a **2.07% real yield** that is unambiguously restrictive, and WTI crude up **+55.8% year-over-year**, which is not a transitory data artifact — it is a sustained cost-of-capital drag on every rate-sensitive pocket of the economy.

Here is where the real tension lives. On one hand, the Sahm Rule reads **0.13%** — nowhere near the recession trigger. The NY Fed recession probability is at **0.44%**. CFNAI has bounced sharply to **+0.14**. Industrial production is positive. These are not recession readings. On the other hand, consumer sentiment has collapsed to **49.80**, a level historically associated with genuine economic distress, and initial claims have jumped **+13%** in a month to **225,000**. You are watching a split-screen: institutional credit says soft landing delivered, the consumer says something is breaking.

The number that deserves your full attention right now is the PPI print: **9.82%**, up **+2.90 pp** in a single month. That is a pipeline-level inflationary impulse that has not yet fully rolled into final goods prices, even as CPI runs at **3.95%** and core CPI holds at **2.99%**. The 10-year breakeven is moving the other way, down **-12 bps** to **2.38%** — markets are calling the PPI spike transitory, a crude-oil artifact. With WTI at **$95.96**, that bet requires energy to reverse hard. If it doesn't, the Fed's ability to hold at **3.62%** effective funds rate gets tested from below, not above.

The most actionable divergence in the global picture is the US-versus-Europe gap. Euro area HICP printed **1.90%** — below target — with core at **2.30%**. The ECB has cut its deposit rate to **2.00%** and its main rate to **2.15%**. That is a central bank loosening into subsiding inflation. The Fed is doing the opposite: sitting at **3.62%** with inflation still running nearly **4%** and real yields punishingly positive. The dollar trade-weighted index at **118.88** barely moved last month (-0.18%), but the policy divergence is real and widening. Bund yields at **3.06%** versus Treasuries at **4.46%** represent a **140 bps** spread that has to resolve — either European growth picks up and forces Bunds higher, or US growth cracks and pulls Treasuries down.

The read for risk assets over the next one to three months is asymmetrically cautious — call it **55/45 toward further compression in risk premium** rather than an outright selloff. Credit is too clean for that. But the regime score at 10/100 tells you the macro tailwinds are gone, and you are riding momentum in the absence of macro support. The thesis flips bearish if PPI feeds through to core CPI, forcing a Fed hawkish surprise; if WTI holds above $90 into Q3; or if claims continue climbing and the Sahm Rule starts creeping toward 0.30%. The Google Trends composite at 0.0 means no retail panic, no contrarian buy signal — the public is not frightened. That is not a bullish setup when fundamentals are deteriorating quietly.
